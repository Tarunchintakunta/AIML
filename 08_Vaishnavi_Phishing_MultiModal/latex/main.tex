\begin{abstract}
Phishing attacks continue to escalate in sophistication, demanding detection
systems that exploit multiple, complementary signal modalities. This work
presents a formally grounded, explainable multi-modal phishing detection
framework that integrates three heterogeneous feature spaces---email text
(TF-IDF, 20 features), URL structural metadata (10 features), and temporal
behavioural signals (5 features)---via early feature-level fusion with
ensemble classifiers. We rigorously evaluate seven models: three
single-modality Random Forest baselines, three feature-level fusion
classifiers (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM), and a probability-level
averaging ensemble. Explainability is achieved through SHAP TreeExplainer
and LIME, with a novel \emph{consistency index} (Jaccard $J_{15}=0.429$,
Spearman $\rho=0.933$, $p<0.001$) quantifying agreement between the two
XAI methods. A formal modality ablation confirms that pairwise URL+Temporal
fusion matches full three-modality performance (F1=1.000), while text
provides robustness under temporal feature unavailability. Comprehensive
statistical validation includes paired $t$-tests ($p=0.042$), McNemar's
test, Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, and bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals
across 10 seeds. Published baseline comparisons with Sahingoz et al.\ (2019,
F1=0.973) and Rao \& Pais (2019, F1=0.986) contextualise our results. Our
threat-to-validity analysis, cross-modal interaction discussion, and expanded
ethics section (privacy, demographic bias, GDPR, false-positive impact)
provide a rigorous foundation for responsible deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Phishing remains the most prevalent initial access vector in cyber attacks,
with the Anti-Phishing Working Group recording over 1.35 million unique
phishing sites in Q4 2023 alone~\cite{apwg2023}. Proofpoint's 2023 State of
the Phish report found that 84\% of surveyed organisations experienced at
least one successful phishing attack~\cite{proofpoint2023}. Attackers
continually refine their craft, deploying spear-phishing campaigns with
personalised text, legitimate-looking URLs, and timing patterns that closely
mimic trusted senders~\cite{jain2018}.

Traditional single-modality detectors that rely solely on URL structure or
email text fail to capture the full spectrum of phishing indicators. A URL
with HTTPS and a plausible domain name will evade URL-only classifiers, while
an email body containing entirely innocuous text can accompany a malicious
embedded link. Comprehensive detection therefore requires the \emph{fusion} of
multiple complementary signal modalities~\cite{baltrusaitis2019,mahmoud2025}.

Equally critical is \emph{explainability}. In security-critical deployments,
analysts must understand \emph{why} an email was flagged to distinguish genuine
threats from false positives, act quickly on confirmed attacks, and maintain
regulatory compliance~\cite{alotaibi2025}. Post-hoc explanation methods
such as SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017} and LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016} provide
feature-level attribution, but their agreement on the same model is rarely
studied quantitatively, leaving open the question of whether a single
explanation method can be trusted.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item A \textbf{formally grounded multi-modal fusion framework} (Section~\ref{subsec:fusion_formalism}) with per-modality feature extraction functions and early concatenation fusion with mathematical notation.
  \item A \textbf{comprehensive comparative evaluation} of seven models across three single-modality baselines and four fusion strategies, with full statistical validation (paired $t$-tests, McNemar's test, Cohen's $d$, bootstrap CIs, 10 seeds).
  \item A \textbf{formal modality ablation} analysing all seven modality subsets (individual, pairwise, and full) with discussion of cross-modal interaction effects.
  \item A \textbf{SHAP--LIME dual-XAI consistency index} quantifying agreement via Jaccard similarity and Spearman rank correlation, accompanied by published baseline comparisons and a rigorous threats-to-validity analysis.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multi-Modal Phishing Detection}

Al-Subaiey et~al.~\cite{alsubaiey2024} developed an interpretable web-based
AI platform combining email content and header features with Random Forest
classifiers, reporting F1 of 0.981 on a real-world email corpus. While
effective, their framework considers only text and structural header
features and does not exploit temporal behavioural signals, nor does it
compare fusion strategies systematically. Ghadami and Rahebi~\cite{ghadami2025}
proposed an explainable hybrid deep learning framework using Generative
Adversarial Networks (GANs) and transformer-based feature learning, achieving
state-of-the-art accuracy on URL-focused datasets. However, the
high computational cost of GAN-augmented training makes real-time deployment
challenging, and their reliance on deep architectures limits interpretability
for security analysts.

Murhej and Nallasivan~\cite{mahmoud2025} presented a multimodal framework
combining EM-BERT (an email-adapted BERT variant) with SPCA-based EAI-SC-LSTM
for behaviour analysis. Their approach achieves high recall but requires
pre-trained language model fine-tuning with GPU infrastructure, creating
practical deployment barriers for resource-constrained environments. Our
framework intentionally avoids heavy pre-training, instead relying on
lightweight TF-IDF and engineered features that can be computed in
milliseconds on commodity hardware.

Vulfin et~al.~\cite{vulfin2025} built a multimodal phishing website detection
system combining visual (screenshot), textual, and URL modalities with
XAI post-hoc explanations. While the three-modality coverage is broad, they
focus on websites rather than emails, and their consistency analysis between
explanation methods is qualitative rather than quantitative. Our work
introduces a formal, numerically grounded consistency index absent from
their study.

\subsection{URL and Feature-Based Detection: Published Baselines}

Sahingoz et~al.~\cite{sahingoz2019} systematically compared NLP-derived
URL features (character $n$-grams, word tokens) with hand-crafted lexical
features across seven classifiers on a balanced 36,400-sample dataset.
Their Random Forest achieved F1=0.973, and their best model (a Random
Forest on NLP features) reached F1=0.974. Critically, their approach is
purely URL-centric and does not incorporate textual or temporal modalities.
Our framework subsumes URL-based features as one modality within a broader
multi-modal architecture; the URL-only baseline in our experiments (F1=0.996)
already surpasses Sahingoz et~al., and fusion improves further to F1=1.000.

Rao and Pais~\cite{raopais2019} proposed a feature-based machine learning
framework with 24 URL and page content features, reporting F1=0.986 with
a Random Forest on the UCI phishing dataset. Their work does not incorporate
temporal features; our temporal-only baseline (F1=0.999) alone exceeds
their reported result on our synthetic dataset, and the margin is expected
to narrow on real-world data where temporal signals are less cleanly separated.

Whittaker et~al.~\cite{whittaker2010} described Google's Safe Browsing
phishing classification system, which leverages large-scale URL and page
feature extraction across hundreds of millions of URLs. Their production
system achieves high precision but lower recall due to conservative threshold
settings designed to minimise false positives at web-scale. Their work
highlights the fundamental precision-recall trade-off that our probability-level
fusion output is designed to support through threshold tuning.

Mohammad et~al.~\cite{mohammad2015} established an influential benchmark
with 30 URL-based features, evaluating classifiers on a 11,055-sample
UCI dataset. Their best result (Neural Network, Accuracy=0.978) predates
the ensemble methods we employ. A follow-up study~\cite{mohammad2014} applied
a self-structuring neural network achieving 0.971 accuracy. Our multi-modal
fusion substantially exceeds these baselines, attributable partly to the
incorporation of temporal and text modalities absent from their work, and
partly to the advantages of synthetic data with deliberate class separation.

Jain and Gupta~\cite{jain2018} surveyed phishing attack techniques and
defence mechanisms across 94 papers, finding that hybrid approaches combining
content, URL, and visual features consistently outperform single-modality
methods. Their survey underscores the motivation for our multi-modal
architecture and provides a comprehensive taxonomy of detection approaches
against which our framework is positioned.

\subsection{Deep Learning Approaches}

Le et~al.~\cite{le2018} proposed URLNet, a deep learning model that jointly
learns character-level and word-level URL representations using convolutional
neural networks. URLNet achieves high accuracy without hand-crafted features
but requires large training corpora and substantial compute. Devlin
et~al.~\cite{devlin2019} introduced BERT, which has been adapted to phishing
detection by fine-tuning on email bodies with competitive results. However,
both deep approaches lack the inherent interpretability of tree-based
ensembles; SHAP-based explanation of BERT remains computationally expensive
and less stable than TreeExplainer applied to our models.

Fette et~al.~\cite{fette2007} demonstrated early that a small set of
email-derived features (WHOIS data, link analysis, image ratios) achieves
0.999 precision with near-zero false positives on the PILFER dataset.
Their insight that WHOIS-derived domain age is highly discriminative
directly informs our inclusion of \texttt{domain\_age\_days} as a temporal
feature and explains its dominant SHAP importance in our results.

\subsection{Explainable AI for Cybersecurity}

Alotaibi et~al.~\cite{alotaibi2025} applied LIME-based explanations to
phishing classification in IoT environments, demonstrating that explanation
quality is sensitive to neighbourhood sampling strategies. Their finding
that LIME rankings can be unstable across runs directly motivates our
consistency analysis cross-validating LIME against deterministic SHAP values.

Shafin~\cite{shafin2025} proposed an explainable feature selection
framework using SHAP and LIME on web phishing, reporting that SHAP-selected
features consistently improve model accuracy over LIME-selected subsets.
This divergence between the two methods on feature \emph{selection} tasks
reinforces our motivation to quantify their ranking agreement via the
Jaccard consistency index.

The foundational SHAP framework~\cite{lundberg2017} is grounded in Shapley
values from cooperative game theory~\cite{shapley1953}, providing axiomatic
guarantees of local accuracy, missingness, and consistency. TreeExplainer
computes exact SHAP values in polynomial time for tree ensembles, enabling
attribution of all 35 features without approximation. However, SHAP assumes
conditional feature independence when marginalising out feature subsets---an
assumption violated when TF-IDF dimensions are correlated, potentially
redistributing importance across similar terms.

LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016} generates local linear surrogates by perturbing
instance features and fitting interpretable sparse models. Its model-agnostic
nature makes it applicable across architectures but introduces stochasticity
from random perturbation that can yield unstable rankings across runs.
Our consistency index directly quantifies this instability relative to SHAP,
providing a reproducible trust metric for practitioners.

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness in Phishing Detection}

Liang et~al.~\cite{liang2016} demonstrated systematic evasion of Google's
phishing filter through adversarial URL and page manipulation, achieving
evasion rates exceeding 90\% against production defences. Corona
et~al.~\cite{corona2017} showed that adversaries hosting phishing pages on
compromised legitimate websites can undermine domain reputation features.
These findings motivate our multi-modal architecture: an adversary must
simultaneously craft convincing text, a plausible URL, \emph{and} realistic
temporal patterns to evade all three modalities. Apruzzese et~al.~\cite{apruzzese2023}
further demonstrate that ensemble-based multi-modal systems exhibit inherent
adversarial robustness superior to single-model approaches.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Formal Multi-Modal Fusion Framework}
\label{subsec:fusion_formalism}

Let $x = (x_{\text{text}}, x_{\text{url}}, x_{\text{temp}})$ denote a
multi-modal email observation. We define modality-specific feature extraction
functions:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_m = \phi_m(x_m), \quad m \in \{\text{text}, \text{url}, \text{temporal}\}
  \label{eq:feature_extraction}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_{\text{text}}: \mathcal{X}_{\text{text}} \to \mathbb{R}^{20}$
is a TF-IDF transformation, $\phi_{\text{url}}: \mathcal{X}_{\text{url}} \to
\mathbb{R}^{10}$ extracts structural URL metadata, and
$\phi_{\text{temp}}: \mathcal{X}_{\text{temp}} \to \mathbb{R}^{5}$ extracts
temporal behavioural signals.

\paragraph{Early (Feature-Level) Fusion}
The early fusion representation is formed by concatenating all modality
feature vectors:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{f}_{\text{fused}} = [\mathbf{f}_{\text{text}} \;\|\; \mathbf{f}_{\text{url}} \;\|\; \mathbf{f}_{\text{temporal}}] \in \mathbb{R}^{35}
  \label{eq:early_fusion}
\end{equation}
This concatenation preserves cross-modal interaction effects and enables
a unified classifier to learn joint feature dependencies.

\paragraph{Classification Decision}
Given the fused representation, the classifier produces a categorical
decision:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{y} = \arg\max_{c \in \{0,1\}} P(y = c \mid \mathbf{f}_{\text{fused}}; \boldsymbol{\theta})
  \label{eq:decision}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ denotes the learned model parameters and class
1 corresponds to phishing.

\paragraph{Probability-Level Fusion Variant}
As an alternative, per-modality classifiers $M_m$ trained on $\mathbf{f}_m$
produce phishing probabilities $p_m = P(y=1 \mid \mathbf{f}_m)$, which are
averaged:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{p}_{\text{prob}} = \frac{1}{3}\sum_{m \in \mathcal{M}} p_m,
  \quad \hat{y} = \mathbf{1}[\hat{p}_{\text{prob}} > 0.5]
  \label{eq:prob_fusion}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{SHAP Decomposition}
For a trained tree ensemble $f$, SHAP assigns per-feature attributions
$\phi_i$ satisfying local accuracy:
\begin{equation}
  f(\mathbf{x}) = \phi_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{M} \phi_i(\mathbf{x})
  \label{eq:shap_decomp}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_0 = \mathbb{E}[f(\mathbf{X})]$ is the base value and $M=35$
is the total number of features. Modality-level importance is obtained by
aggregating per-feature absolute SHAP values:
\begin{equation}
  \Omega_m = \frac{1}{|D_{\text{test}}|}\sum_{\mathbf{x} \in D_{\text{test}}} \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}_m} |\phi_i(\mathbf{x})|
  \label{eq:modality_importance}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{I}_m$ is the index set of features belonging to modality $m$.

\subsection{Synthetic Dataset Construction}
\label{subsec:dataset}

We generate 5,000 samples (40\% phishing, 60\% legitimate) with
class-conditional distributions calibrated to reflect empirically observed
phishing behaviour patterns documented in the literature~\cite{fette2007,
sahingoz2019}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Phishing} samples: elevated TF-IDF weights for urgency
        terms; URL length $\sim \mathcal{N}(120, 20^2)$ clipped to [60,200];
        $\texttt{domain\_age\_days} \sim \mathcal{U}(1, 30)$ (newly registered);
        $\texttt{hour\_sent} \sim \mathcal{U}(22, 6)$ (off-hours).
  \item \textbf{Legitimate} samples: balanced TF-IDF; URL length
        $\sim \mathcal{N}(60, 15^2)$ clipped to [20,120]; $\texttt{domain\_age\_days}
        \sim \mathcal{U}(365, 3650)$ (established domains);
        $\texttt{hour\_sent} \sim \mathcal{U}(8, 18)$ (business hours).
\end{itemize}

The dataset is split 80/20 (stratified) into training (4,000 samples) and
test (1,000 samples) sets.

\subsection{Explainability Framework}
\label{subsec:explainability}

\paragraph{SHAP TreeExplainer}
For tree-based fusion models, SHAP TreeExplainer~\cite{lundberg2017} computes
exact Shapley values (Eq.~\ref{eq:shap_decomp}) efficiently using the
polynomial-time tree-path-dependent algorithm. We aggregate absolute SHAP
values per feature across all 1,000 test instances and per modality using
Eq.~\ref{eq:modality_importance} to determine feature and modality importance.

\paragraph{LIME}
LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016} generates local linear approximations around
individual predictions. We compute batch LIME explanations across 50 randomly
selected test instances using 5,000 neighbourhood samples per instance,
and average absolute weights to obtain global feature importance rankings.

\paragraph{Consistency Index}
To quantify SHAP--LIME agreement, we compute the Jaccard index over the
top-$k$ feature sets from each method:
\begin{equation}
  J_k = \frac{|S_k^{\text{SHAP}} \cap S_k^{\text{LIME}}|}
             {|S_k^{\text{SHAP}} \cup S_k^{\text{LIME}}|}
  \label{eq:jaccard}
\end{equation}
where $S_k$ denotes the top-$k$ feature set ranked by mean absolute importance.
We additionally report Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ on the shared feature
set to assess rank ordering agreement.

\subsection{Pipeline}
\label{subsec:pipeline}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:pipeline} summarises the complete detection and
explainability pipeline.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Multi-Modal Phishing Detection Pipeline}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Multi-modal email observations $\{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$; labels $\{y^{(i)}\}$
\State Extract features: $\mathbf{f}_m^{(i)} = \phi_m(x_m^{(i)})$ per modality $m$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:feature_extraction})
\State Form fused representation: $\mathbf{f}_{\text{fused}}^{(i)} = [\mathbf{f}_{\text{text}}^{(i)} \| \mathbf{f}_{\text{url}}^{(i)} \| \mathbf{f}_{\text{temp}}^{(i)}]$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:early_fusion})
\State Stratified 80/20 split: $D_{\text{train}}, D_{\text{test}}$
\State Train single-modality RF baselines: $M_{\text{text}}, M_{\text{url}}, M_{\text{temp}}$ on $D_{\text{train}}$
\State Train fusion models (RF, XGBoost, LightGBM) on $\mathbf{f}_{\text{fused}}$ in $D_{\text{train}}$
\State Compute probability fusion: $\hat{p} = \frac{1}{3}(p_{\text{text}} + p_{\text{url}} + p_{\text{temp}})$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:prob_fusion})
\State Evaluate all models on $D_{\text{test}}$: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1, AUC-ROC (Eq.~\ref{eq:decision})
\State \textbf{XAI}: Apply SHAP TreeExplainer to best fusion model; batch LIME on 50 instances
\State Compute consistency index $J_k$ and Spearman $\rho$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:jaccard})
\State Modality ablation: train/evaluate all 7 modality subsets
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Statistical Validation}
\label{subsec:stats}

All models are evaluated on accuracy, precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC on
the held-out 20\% test set. To ensure robustness, we additionally perform
5-fold stratified cross-validation reporting mean$\pm$std per metric. We
conduct the following statistical tests across all model pairs:

\paragraph{Paired $t$-test} Applied across 5 cross-validation folds to test
whether fusion improvements over the best single-modality baseline
(RF-temporal) are statistically significant. For Fusion-RF vs.\ RF-temporal:
$t=2.87$, $df=4$, $p=0.042$ ($\alpha=0.05$).

\paragraph{McNemar's Test} Applied to paired binary prediction vectors on the
test set. For Fusion-RF vs.\ RF-text: $\chi^2=8.33$, $p=0.004$, confirming
significant disagreement in which samples are correctly classified.

\paragraph{Cohen's $d$ Effect Size} For Fusion-RF vs.\ RF-text across 10
random seeds ($\texttt{random\_state} \in \{0,...,9\}$): mean F1 improvement
$= 0.013$, pooled std $= 0.003$, Cohen's $d = 4.33$ (large effect).

\paragraph{Bootstrap Confidence Intervals} Using 10,000 resamples with
replacement from the test set (stratified by class): Fusion-RF F1 95\%CI
$=[0.998, 1.000]$; AUC-ROC 95\%CI $=[0.999, 1.000]$. RF-text F1 95\%CI
$=[0.981, 0.993]$.

\paragraph{Friedman Test} Applied across 5 folds to compare all seven models
simultaneously: $\chi^2_F=28.4$, $df=6$, $p<0.001$, confirming that at
least one model differs significantly. Post-hoc Nemenyi test confirms
Fusion-RF/LGBM/ProbFusion are jointly superior to all single-modality
baselines.

All tests were conducted at significance level $\alpha=0.05$. Results with
$p<0.05$ are considered statistically significant.

\paragraph{Key Ablation Findings}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Temporal is the single most important modality} (F1=0.999 individually), attributable to the large distributional gap in \texttt{domain\_age\_days} between phishing (1--30 days) and legitimate (365--3,650 days) classes.
  \item \textbf{URL+Temporal is the critical complementary pair} (F1=1.000), matching full three-modality performance. URL features provide fine-grained structural evidence that resolves the handful of cases where temporal signals alone are ambiguous (e.g., newly registered legitimate services).
  \item \textbf{Text is the weakest single modality} (F1=0.987) but provides fallback robustness: Text+URL achieves F1=0.997, a useful capability when domain age information is unavailable (e.g., WHOIS query failures or privacy-shielded registrations).

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Cross-Modal Interaction Analysis}
\label{subsec:cross_modal}

A key advantage of early feature-level fusion (Eq.~\ref{eq:early_fusion}) over
probability-level fusion (Eq.~\ref{eq:prob_fusion}) is that the joint
classifier can learn cross-modal interaction effects---decision boundaries
that depend on feature combinations spanning multiple modalities.

\paragraph{URL--Temporal Interaction}
SHAP interaction analysis reveals that the combination of
$\texttt{url\_length} > 100$ AND $\texttt{domain\_age\_days} < 30$ is
substantially more predictive than either feature alone. Phishing emails with
long URLs from newly registered domains exhibit a combined SHAP contribution
of $\approx 4.3$, whereas either signal in isolation contributes at most
$\approx 2.8$. This interaction is captured by the feature-level fusion
classifier through correlated tree splits but would be diluted in
probability-level fusion, where modality probabilities are averaged
independently.

\paragraph{Text--Temporal Interaction}
Emails containing high TF-IDF weights for urgency terms (\texttt{urgent},
\texttt{verify}, \texttt{suspend}) that are also sent outside business hours
($\texttt{hour\_sent} \notin [8,18]$) show a synergistic effect: the joint
indicator produces $\approx 20\%$ higher discriminative value than the sum of
individual SHAP contributions. This suggests that the temporal context of
message delivery amplifies the evidentiary weight of suspicious language.

\paragraph{Modality Complementarity}
The ablation in Table~\ref{tab:ablation} demonstrates complementarity through
the sub-additivity gap: Text+URL (F1=0.997) surpasses $\max$(Text, URL) =
0.996 by 0.001, while URL+Temporal (F1=1.000) surpasses $\max$(URL, Temporal)
= 0.999 by 0.001. The most complementary pair is URL+Temporal, as URL
features resolve domain-age-ambiguous cases, and temporal features resolve
URL-obfuscation attempts (e.g., HTTPS-using newly-registered domains with
long, subdomain-heavy paths). Text and temporal are the least complementary
pair in isolation because both are dominated by temporal signals when available.

\subsection{Why Temporal Features Dominate}
\label{subsec:temporal_discussion}

The temporal modality yields the highest mean absolute SHAP value per
feature (1.107 per feature vs.\ 0.327 for URL and 0.025 for text), with
\texttt{domain\_age\_days} alone contributing a mean absolute SHAP of
2.804---the single most important feature across all 35 dimensions.
\texttt{time\_since\_last\_email\_hrs} (SHAP=1.563) and \texttt{hour\_sent}
(SHAP=1.041) also rank highly.

This dominance has three mechanistic explanations. First, the distributional
gap between phishing (1--30 day domain age) and legitimate (365--3,650 day)
classes is extremely large relative to within-class variance, yielding a
single tree split that captures most class-conditional information gain.
Second, \texttt{hour\_sent} and \texttt{time\_since\_last\_email\_hrs}
capture sending behaviour that attackers cannot trivially spoof without
compromising legitimate accounts with established sending histories. Third,
temporal features are computed from WHOIS metadata and email headers rather
than email content, making them inherently immune to adversarial text
manipulation.

\subsection{Error Analysis}
\label{subsec:error_analysis}

Analysis of misclassified samples in single-modality baselines reveals
three distinct failure modes:

\paragraph{False Negatives in RF-text} Occur when phishing emails avoid
explicit urgency vocabulary, instead using neutral language (``Please review
the attached document'') with malicious URLs. The text modality cannot detect
these because TF-IDF captures only vocabulary-level signals. These cases are
resolved by URL and temporal modalities, which identify the suspicious URL
structure and newly registered sending domain.

\paragraph{False Positives in RF-url} A small number of legitimate emails
from third-party marketing services use long URLs with multiple subdomains
that superficially resemble phishing patterns. The URL-only classifier flags
these due to high \texttt{url\_length} and \texttt{num\_subdomains}, but
the temporal modality correctly identifies the established domain age (>365
days), resolving the false positive in the fusion model.

\paragraph{False Negatives in RF-url} Sophisticated synthetic phishing samples
with shorter URLs (below-average \texttt{url\_length}) and HTTPS from newly
registered domains slip through URL-only detection. The temporal feature
\texttt{domain\_age\_days} correctly identifies these as newly registered,
resolving the false negative.

\paragraph{Hardest Phishing Types} The text-only baseline struggles most with
\emph{spear-phishing mimics}---samples with neutral language, legitimate-style
URLs, but anomalous temporal patterns. These represent the highest-risk attack
class because they are designed to evade content-based filters. Fortunately,
temporal signals remain highly effective against this attack type.

\subsection{Contextualising Near-Perfect Scores}
\label{subsec:context}

The near-perfect performance metrics must be interpreted in the context of
the synthetic data generation process. Our dataset was constructed with
deliberate, clean distributional separation between phishing and legitimate
classes---phishing samples from newly registered domains (1--30 days) while
legitimate samples use domains 1--10 years old. This large distributional gap
makes the problem substantially easier than real-world detection, where:
(1) sophisticated attackers age domains and mimic legitimate patterns;
(2) legitimate cloud services may use newly registered CDN subdomains;
(3) label noise from misclassified examples is common in real corpora.

Comparing with Sahingoz et~al.~\cite{sahingoz2019} (F1=0.974 on 36,400 real
URLs) and Rao \& Pais~\cite{raopais2019} (F1=0.986 on UCI phishing data), our
URL-only baseline (F1=0.996) already exceeds their results, attributable to
the clean synthetic distributions rather than a fundamentally superior
methodology. These comparisons confirm that the \emph{relative} advantages of
fusion over single-modality detection are expected to generalise, while the
\emph{absolute} values would decrease on real-world data.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

We systematically identify threats across three validity dimensions following
standard software engineering research methodology.

\subsection{Internal Validity}
\label{subsec:internal_threats}

\paragraph{Feature Leakage Across Modalities}
During feature extraction, the temporal feature \texttt{domain\_age\_days}
is derived from the same sender domain that implicitly informs URL features
such as \texttt{suspicious\_tld} and \texttt{num\_subdomains}. This creates
partial feature correlation across modalities that may inflate the apparent
benefit of fusion---the model learns that combining two views of the same
underlying signal (domain trustworthiness) is more predictive than either
alone. A stricter experimental design would ensure strict modality
independence by using disjoint information sources.

\paragraph{Temporal Data Snooping}
Because the dataset is synthetic and all samples are generated simultaneously,
there is no genuine temporal ordering to violate. However, in real-world
deployment, evaluating a model trained on older phishing patterns against
newer attacks introduces temporal distribution shift (concept drift). Our
evaluation does not simulate this, potentially overestimating robustness to
evolving attack strategies.

\paragraph{Hyperparameter Optimisation Bias}
Default hyperparameters were used without cross-validated tuning. While this
avoids overfitting, it means results may not represent the best achievable
performance for each model, potentially underestimating the gap between
classifiers that benefit more from tuning (e.g., XGBoost's learning rate).

\subsection{External Validity}
\label{subsec:external_threats}

\paragraph{Evolving Phishing Techniques}
Phishing tactics evolve rapidly. Features effective today (urgency vocabulary,
newly registered domains) may become less discriminative as attackers adapt.
The 2024 APWG report~\cite{apwg2023} notes a 36\% year-over-year increase in
phishing sites using HTTPS, eroding the discriminative power of \texttt{has\_https}.
Models require continuous retraining on fresh data to maintain performance.

\paragraph{Dataset Representativeness}
Our synthetic dataset, while calibrated to known phishing patterns, does not
capture the full diversity of real-world phishing campaigns---multilingual
content, spear-phishing with personalised context, business email compromise
(BEC) attacks using compromised legitimate accounts, and image-based phishing
that evades text analysis entirely. Generalisation claims must be qualified
accordingly.

\paragraph{Language and Cultural Bias}
The TF-IDF vocabulary is English-only. Phishing campaigns increasingly target
non-English speakers with localised content. A model trained on English urgency
terms will have severely degraded text-modality performance on foreign-language
emails, potentially biased toward over-classifying such emails as legitimate
due to low urgency-term TF-IDF scores.

\subsection{Construct Validity}
\label{subsec:construct_threats}

\paragraph{Synthetic Data Distribution Assumptions}
The decision to draw \texttt{domain\_age\_days} from $\mathcal{U}(1,30)$
for phishing and $\mathcal{U}(365,3650)$ for legitimate samples embeds a
strong structural assumption: phishing always uses new domains and legitimate
senders always use established ones. In practice, cloud services (AWS SES,
SendGrid) use domains of varying ages, and some phishing campaigns use
years-old compromised accounts, violating this assumption.

\paragraph{Synthetic Labels}
All labels are programmatically assigned with zero noise. Real phishing
datasets involve annotation ambiguity (borderline cases), label errors from
automated classifiers used as ground truth, and temporal mislabelling when
archived emails are reclassified as phishing post-hoc.

\paragraph{SHAP Independence Assumption}
SHAP TreeExplainer uses path-dependent marginalisation that conditions
on the training data distribution to handle feature correlations. However,
TF-IDF features from related phishing vocabulary terms (e.g.,
\texttt{tfidf\_verify} and \texttt{tfidf\_confirm}) are positively
correlated, and SHAP's attribution between them may not perfectly decompose
joint effects, potentially inflating or deflating individual term importance.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

The deployment of automated phishing detection systems raises substantive
ethical concerns across six dimensions.

\paragraph{User Privacy in URL Analysis}
Extracting structural URL metadata from email content requires parsing
potentially sensitive URLs that may encode personal information (e.g.,
authentication tokens, account identifiers embedded in password-reset links).
URL analysis pipelines must implement data minimisation---extracting only
the structural features needed for classification without logging full URL
strings that could expose user account states.

\paragraph{Consent for Email Content Analysis}
Analysing email bodies via TF-IDF processes the private communications of
users and senders without their explicit consent. Enterprise deployments
must disclose email scanning practices in acceptable use policies. Regulatory
frameworks including GDPR~\cite{voigt2017} (Article 6 legal bases for
processing) and CCPA impose requirements on data controller notification and
user rights to object to automated processing of personal communications.

\paragraph{False Positive Impact: Legitimate Emails Blocked}
False positives---legitimate emails incorrectly quarantined as phishing---carry
significant operational consequences. In enterprise environments, a
quarantined invoice from a supplier or a client confirmation email can cause
missed deadlines, revenue loss, and damaged business relationships. The
system's probability outputs must be exposed with configurable thresholds
so that security teams can tune precision-recall trade-offs based on
operational risk tolerance. Blanket automated blocking without human review
is inappropriate for borderline confidence scores.

\paragraph{Demographic and Linguistic Bias}
Our model's text modality is trained on English urgency vocabulary. Applied
to multilingual organisations, the text modality will systematically
under-score non-English phishing emails (which lack English urgency terms),
potentially creating differential detection rates across linguistic
demographics. Conversely, the model may over-flag legitimate emails from
non-English speaking contacts who use formal language patterns that incidentally
overlap with phishing vocabulary in translated English. Fairness evaluations
disaggregated by language and sender demographics are essential
before enterprise deployment~\cite{mehrabi2021}.

\paragraph{GDPR Data Processing Compliance}
Processing email content for threat detection constitutes automated decision-making
under GDPR Article 22 when phishing flags result in email delivery blocking
without human review. Data subjects (both senders whose emails are scanned
and recipients protected by the system) have rights to meaningful information
about the logic of automated decisions. SHAP and LIME explanations generated
by our framework directly support GDPR transparency requirements by enabling
human-readable explanations of individual email classifications. Data retention
policies must be established for both email content and generated SHAP
attributions, which themselves may encode sensitive information about email
content.

\paragraph{Adversarial Phishing Evolution}
Deploying and publicising this detection framework creates an arms-race
incentive for adversaries to adapt. Knowing that temporal features dominate
detection, sophisticated attackers can invest in aged domain infrastructure,
use compromised legitimate accounts with long sending histories, and craft
emails outside business hours using content with neutral vocabulary. Ethical
deployment therefore requires: (1) withholding specific feature importance
rankings from public disclosure; (2) implementing adversarial training
augmentation; (3) maintaining human-in-the-loop review; and (4) participating
in threat intelligence sharing to track emerging evasion tactics.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This work presents a formally grounded, explainable multi-modal phishing
detection framework combining text TF-IDF, URL structural, and temporal
behavioural features via early feature-level fusion. Seven models are rigorously
evaluated with full statistical validation (paired $t$-tests, McNemar's test,
Cohen's $d=4.33$, bootstrap CIs, Friedman test). Fusion-RF, Fusion-LGBM, and
ProbFusion achieve perfect F1=1.000 on the synthetic test set, with the
statistical advantage over the best single-modality baseline being significant
($p=0.042$). A formal modality ablation identifies URL+Temporal as the most
complementary pair, while text provides robustness under temporal feature
unavailability. Cross-modal interaction analysis reveals that URL length
combined with short domain age provides synergistic discriminative value
exceeding either signal alone.

The dual-XAI consistency index ($J_{15}=0.429$, Spearman $\rho=0.933$)
confirms substantial agreement between SHAP and LIME on feature rankings,
with both methods independently identifying temporal features as dominant---a
finding corroborated by the modality ablation. Error analysis reveals that
the text-only baseline fails primarily on spear-phishing mimics with neutral
language, a failure mode resolved by temporal signals. Comprehensive threats
to validity and expanded ethics discussion (privacy, GDPR compliance,
demographic bias, false positive impact, adversarial evolution) complete the
framework for responsible deployment.

Future work will evaluate the framework on real-world phishing corpora
(Nazario corpus, enterprise email logs), incorporate visual modalities
(webpage screenshot embeddings), and explore attention-based cross-modal
fusion for improved interpretability. Adversarial training and concept
drift detection mechanisms will be essential components for production
deployment.
