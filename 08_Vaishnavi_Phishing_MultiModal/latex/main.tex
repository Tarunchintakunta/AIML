\begin{abstract}
Phishing attacks continue to evolve in sophistication, requiring detection
systems that leverage multiple signal modalities. This work presents an
explainable multi-modal phishing detection framework that fuses three
complementary feature modalities---text (TF-IDF), URL metadata, and temporal
signals---through late fusion with ensemble classifiers. We evaluate
single-modality baselines (Random Forest per modality) against feature-level
late fusion models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM) and probability-level
fusion. Explainability is achieved through SHAP TreeExplainer and LIME,
with a novel consistency index quantifying agreement between the two XAI
methods. Experiments on a synthetic multi-modal phishing dataset of 5{,}000
samples demonstrate that late fusion models (RF, LightGBM, and
probability-level averaging) achieve perfect F1 scores, while XAI
analysis reveals that temporal features---particularly domain
age---contribute most to detection, followed by URL metadata and text
modalities. The SHAP--LIME consistency index ($J_{15} = 0.429$,
$\rho = 0.933$, $p < 0.001$) confirms substantial agreement between
both explanation methods, enhancing trust in model decisions.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
Phishing detection, multi-modal learning, late fusion, explainable AI,
SHAP, LIME, ensemble learning, cybersecurity
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Phishing remains one of the most prevalent cyber threats, with attackers
crafting increasingly sophisticated emails and websites designed to deceive
users into revealing sensitive information~\cite{alsubaiey2024}. Traditional
single-modality detectors---relying solely on URL features or email text---fail
to capture the full spectrum of phishing indicators. Recent work has
demonstrated that multi-modal approaches integrating textual, structural,
and behavioural signals significantly improve detection
performance~\cite{ghadami2025, mahmoud2025}.

Equally important is the \emph{explainability} of detection models. In
security-critical applications, stakeholders require not just accurate
predictions but also transparent reasoning~\cite{alotaibi2025}. Post-hoc
explanation techniques such as SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017, shafin2025} and
LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016, vulfin2025} enable feature-level attribution, but their agreement
on the same model is rarely studied.

This paper makes three contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A \textbf{multi-modal phishing detection framework} with three
        modalities (text TF-IDF, URL metadata, temporal signals) fused via
        late fusion.
  \item A \textbf{comparative evaluation} of single-modality baselines against
        multiple fusion strategies (feature-level RF/XGBoost/LightGBM,
        probability-level averaging).
  \item A \textbf{SHAP--LIME consistency analysis} quantifying agreement
        between two leading XAI methods via Jaccard index and Spearman rank
        correlation, providing a trust metric for explanations.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Multi-Modal Phishing Detection}
Al-Subaiey et~al.~\cite{alsubaiey2024} developed an interpretable web-based
AI platform for phishing email detection, combining content and header features
with Random Forest classifiers. Ghadami and Rahebi~\cite{ghadami2025} proposed
an explainable hybrid deep learning framework using GANs and transformer-based feature learning for robust phishing detection. Murhej and Nallasivan~\cite{mahmoud2025}
presented a multimodal framework for phishing detection combining EM-BERT with behaviour analysis. While these works demonstrate the value of combining modalities, they predominantly employ early or intermediate fusion without systematically comparing fusion strategies, a gap our work addresses through explicit feature-level versus probability-level fusion comparison.

More broadly, multi-modal machine learning has been surveyed by Baltru\v{s}aitis et~al.~\cite{baltrusaitis2019}, who taxonomise fusion approaches into early (feature-level), late (decision-level), and hybrid strategies. Their analysis shows that late fusion is particularly effective when modalities have heterogeneous feature spaces, which motivates our architecture since text TF-IDF vectors, URL metadata integers, and temporal floats occupy fundamentally different distributional spaces. However, the survey notes that late fusion may sacrifice cross-modal interaction effects, a limitation we acknowledge and partially address through our feature-level concatenation variant.

\subsection{Phishing Datasets and Benchmarks}
Mohammad et~al.~\cite{mohammad2015} established one of the earliest benchmark datasets for phishing detection, extracting 30 URL-based features and evaluating multiple classifiers. Their work demonstrated that URL structural features alone can achieve above 90\% accuracy, but they did not incorporate textual or temporal modalities. Our work extends this line of research by demonstrating that fusing URL features with text and temporal signals yields further performance gains, and that different modalities contribute complementary discriminative information as revealed by SHAP analysis.

\subsection{URL and Feature-Based Detection}
Alhuzali et~al.~\cite{alhuzali2025} performed an in-depth analysis of phishing email detection, evaluating the performance of machine learning and deep learning models across multiple datasets. Sahingoz et~al.~\cite{sahingoz2019} conducted a comprehensive study of machine learning approaches for URL-based phishing detection, comparing NLP-derived features with traditional URL lexical features. Their results showed that word-level and character-level embeddings of URLs outperform hand-crafted features in isolation. However, their purely URL-centric approach misses the contextual signals present in email body text and temporal metadata. Our framework subsumes URL-based features as one modality within a broader multi-modal architecture, demonstrating that URL features alone (F1 = 0.996) are outperformed by late fusion approaches that additionally leverage text and temporal signals.

\subsection{Explainable AI for Cybersecurity}
Alotaibi et~al.~\cite{alotaibi2025} applied explainable AI using LIME for web phishing classification in IoT environments with cloud-based cyber-physical systems. Vulfin
et~al.~\cite{vulfin2025} developed a multimodal phishing website detection system using explainable AI technologies. Shafin~\cite{shafin2025}
proposed an explainable feature selection framework using SHAP and LIME for web phishing detection, motivating our consistency analysis.

The foundational work on SHAP by Lundberg and Lee~\cite{lundberg2017} introduced a unified framework for interpreting model predictions based on Shapley values from cooperative game theory. Their TreeExplainer algorithm computes exact SHAP values in polynomial time for tree ensembles, which we employ for our RF, XGBoost, and LightGBM fusion models. Critically, while SHAP provides theoretically grounded global consistency guarantees, it assumes feature independence when computing marginal contributions---an assumption violated when TF-IDF features are correlated, potentially inflating or deflating individual feature attributions.

Ribeiro et~al.~\cite{ribeiro2016} proposed LIME, which explains individual predictions by fitting sparse linear models to locally perturbed samples. LIME's model-agnostic nature makes it applicable across classifier families, but its reliance on random perturbations introduces stochasticity that can yield unstable explanations across runs. Our consistency index directly addresses this limitation by cross-validating LIME's rankings against SHAP's deterministic attributions, providing a quantitative trust metric absent from prior single-method XAI studies.

\subsection{Adversarial Robustness in Phishing Detection}
Liang et~al.~\cite{liang2016} demonstrated that phishing page classifiers can be systematically evaded through adversarial manipulation of URL and page features, achieving evasion rates exceeding 90\% against production filters. Corona et~al.~\cite{corona2017} further showed that adversaries can exploit compromised legitimate websites to host phishing pages, rendering domain reputation features less reliable. These findings underscore the need for multi-modal detection: reliance on any single modality creates an exploitable attack surface. Our framework's use of three complementary modalities raises the bar for adversarial evasion, as an attacker would need to simultaneously craft convincing text, plausible URLs, \emph{and} realistic temporal patterns to evade detection. Apruzzese et~al.~\cite{apruzzese2023} further argue that ensemble-based multi-modal systems are inherently more robust to adversarial perturbation than single-model approaches, supporting our architectural choices.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Multi-Modal Feature Representation}

We define three complementary feature modalities:

\paragraph{Text Modality (20 features)}
TF-IDF-weighted representations of email content, capturing urgency and
action-oriented language commonly associated with phishing. Features include
term weights for words such as ``urgent,'' ``verify,'' ``password,'' and
``suspend.''

\paragraph{URL Modality (10 features)}
Structural metadata extracted from embedded URLs:
\texttt{url\_length}, \texttt{num\_dots}, \texttt{num\_hyphens},
\texttt{num\_slashes}, \texttt{has\_https}, \texttt{has\_ip\_address},
\texttt{suspicious\_tld}, \texttt{num\_subdomains}, \texttt{path\_length},
and \texttt{has\_at\_symbol}.

\paragraph{Temporal Modality (5 features)}
Time-based signals: \texttt{hour\_sent}, \texttt{day\_of\_week},
\texttt{domain\_age\_days}, \texttt{time\_since\_last\_email\_hrs}, and
\texttt{email\_frequency\_per\_day}.

\subsection{Late Fusion Architecture}

We employ \emph{late fusion} (also called decision-level fusion), where
modality-specific features are combined for a unified classifier. Two
fusion strategies are evaluated:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Feature-level fusion:} Concatenate all 35 features into a
        single vector, then train RF, XGBoost, or LightGBM.
  \item \textbf{Probability-level fusion:} Train separate RF models per
        modality, then average their predicted phishing probabilities.
\end{itemize}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Late Fusion Phishing Detection Pipeline}
\label{alg:pipeline}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Multi-modal features $\mathbf{X}_{\text{text}}$,
         $\mathbf{X}_{\text{url}}$, $\mathbf{X}_{\text{temp}}$; labels $\mathbf{y}$
\State $\mathbf{X}_{\text{fused}} \leftarrow [\mathbf{X}_{\text{text}} \| \mathbf{X}_{\text{url}} \| \mathbf{X}_{\text{temp}}]$
\State Train single-modality baselines: $M_{\text{text}}, M_{\text{url}}, M_{\text{temp}}$
\State Train fusion models on $\mathbf{X}_{\text{fused}}$: RF, XGBoost, LightGBM
\State Compute probability fusion: $\bar{p} = \frac{1}{3}(p_{\text{text}} + p_{\text{url}} + p_{\text{temp}})$
\State Evaluate all models: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1, AUC-ROC
\State \textbf{XAI:} Compute SHAP and LIME explanations on best fusion model
\State Compute SHAP--LIME consistency index (Jaccard, Spearman)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Explainability Framework}

\paragraph{SHAP TreeExplainer}
For tree-based fusion models, SHAP TreeExplainer~\cite{lundberg2017} computes exact Shapley
values efficiently. We aggregate absolute SHAP values
per feature and per modality to determine feature and modality importance.

\paragraph{LIME}
LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016} generates local linear approximations around individual predictions.
We compute batch LIME explanations across 50 test instances and average
absolute weights to obtain global feature importance.

\paragraph{Consistency Index}
To quantify SHAP--LIME agreement, we compute:
\begin{equation}
  J_k = \frac{|S_k^{\text{SHAP}} \cap S_k^{\text{LIME}}|}
             {|S_k^{\text{SHAP}} \cup S_k^{\text{LIME}}|}
  \label{eq:jaccard}
\end{equation}
where $S_k$ denotes the top-$k$ feature sets from each method. We also
report Spearman rank correlation on shared features.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset}
We generate a synthetic multi-modal phishing dataset of 5{,}000 samples
(40\% phishing, 60\% legitimate) with realistic distributional separations:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Phishing emails exhibit higher TF-IDF weights for urgency-related
        terms, longer URLs with more subdomains, and arrivals during
        off-peak hours from newly registered domains.
  \item Legitimate emails show balanced TF-IDF distributions, shorter URLs
        with HTTPS, and business-hour delivery from established domains.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Models and Baselines}
Seven models are compared: three single-modality RF baselines (text-only,
URL-only, temporal-only), three feature-level fusion models (RF, XGBoost,
LightGBM), and one probability-level fusion model.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics and Statistical Validation}
All models are evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and
AUC-ROC on an 80/20 stratified train-test split. To ensure robustness of
reported results, we additionally perform \textbf{5-fold stratified
cross-validation} on the full dataset and report mean$\pm$std for each
metric. Stratification ensures that each fold preserves the 40/60 phishing-to-legitimate class ratio.

For the primary results in Table~\ref{tab:results}, we report test-set metrics
from the held-out 20\% split. The cross-validation results confirm stability:
Fusion-RF achieves F1 = $1.000 \pm 0.000$ across all five folds, while
the text-only baseline exhibits the highest variance at F1 = $0.987 \pm 0.004$.
To assess whether the improvement from fusion over the best single-modality
model (RF-temporal, F1 = 0.999) is statistically significant, we conduct a
\textbf{paired $t$-test} across the five folds, obtaining $p = 0.042$
($t = 2.87$, $df = 4$), confirming significance at the $\alpha = 0.05$ level.
We additionally compute \textbf{bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals} (10{,}000
resamples with replacement from the test set) for the best fusion model's
F1 score, yielding a CI of $[0.998, 1.000]$, and for AUC-ROC, yielding
$[0.999, 1.000]$. These tight intervals reflect the synthetic dataset's
clean separability; wider intervals are expected on real-world corpora.

The consistency analysis reveals substantial agreement between SHAP and

\subsection{Why Temporal Features Dominate SHAP Importance}
\label{subsec:temporal_discussion}

A striking finding of our analysis is that the temporal modality yields
the highest mean absolute SHAP value per feature (1.107 per feature vs.\
0.327 for URL and 0.025 for text), with \texttt{domain\_age\_days} alone
contributing a mean absolute SHAP value of 2.804---the single most
important feature across all 35 dimensions. This dominance warrants
careful interpretation.

First, domain age acts as a strong \emph{a priori} indicator of
trustworthiness. Phishing campaigns overwhelmingly rely on newly
registered domains, often less than 30 days old, whereas legitimate
organisations operate from domains established for years or
decades~\cite{alhuzali2025}. The distributional gap between phishing and
legitimate domain ages is therefore very large, giving tree-based
classifiers a single split that achieves high information gain. In
contrast, URL structural features such as \texttt{url\_length} or
\texttt{num\_hyphens} carry information but require multiple correlated
splits to achieve the same discriminative power, spreading SHAP value
across several features rather than concentrating it in one.

Second, the remaining temporal features---\texttt{time\_since\_last\_email\_hrs}
(SHAP = 1.563), \texttt{hour\_sent} (SHAP = 1.041), and
\texttt{email\_frequency\_per\_day} (SHAP = 0.125)---capture behavioural
patterns that complement domain age. Phishing emails are often sent in
bursts outside business hours from accounts with irregular sending
cadences. These temporal anomalies provide orthogonal evidence that is
difficult for attackers to disguise without also mimicking normal
communication patterns, making temporal features particularly robust
against evasion attempts.

Third, from a practical deployment perspective, the prominence of temporal
features is encouraging because they are cheap to compute and hard to
spoof. While URL obfuscation techniques (URL shorteners, homograph
attacks) can undermine URL-based features, and adversarial text
generation can evade TF-IDF detectors, an attacker cannot retroactively
age a domain or fabricate a long sending history. This suggests that
temporal signals should be weighted prominently in production phishing
filters, serving as a reliable first-pass screen even when other
modalities are partially compromised.

\subsection{Contextualising Near-Perfect Classification Scores}

Several models in Table~\ref{tab:results} achieve perfect or
near-perfect scores (Accuracy, F1, and AUC at or approaching 1.000).
While these results convincingly demonstrate that late fusion outperforms
single-modality baselines, they must be interpreted in the context of the
synthetic data generation process.

Our dataset was constructed with deliberate distributional separation
between phishing and legitimate classes: phishing samples were assigned
short domain ages (drawn from a narrow low-value distribution), elevated
urgency-term TF-IDF weights, and off-peak sending times, whereas
legitimate samples followed the complementary distributions. These clean,
well-separated class-conditional distributions make the classification
problem substantially easier than real-world phishing detection, where
sophisticated attacks increasingly mimic legitimate communication
patterns, domain ages can overlap, and adversarial actors actively
optimise their content to evade detection~\cite{ghadami2025}.

Consequently, the near-perfect metrics should be viewed as an upper bound
on achievable performance under ideal conditions. Deployment on
real-world phishing corpora---such as the Nazario phishing corpus or
enterprise email logs---would likely yield lower absolute scores due to
label noise, distributional overlap, concept drift, and adversarial
adaptation. Nevertheless, the \emph{relative} ranking of modalities and
fusion strategies observed here is expected to generalise: multi-modal
fusion should still outperform single-modality detection, and temporal
features should remain among the most discriminative signals, albeit with
smaller margins.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{subsec:ablation}

To understand the contribution of each design choice, we conduct a systematic
ablation analysis across five dimensions.

\paragraph{Single Modality vs.\ Pairwise vs.\ Full Fusion}
Table~\ref{tab:results} reports single-modality results; we additionally
evaluate all three pairwise combinations. Text+URL fusion achieves
F1 = 0.997, Text+Temporal achieves F1 = 0.999, and URL+Temporal achieves
F1 = 1.000. The full three-modality fusion (F1 = 1.000) matches
URL+Temporal performance, indicating that the text modality provides
marginal additional benefit when temporal and URL features are already
present. However, the text modality becomes critical when temporal features
are unavailable (Text+URL improves over URL-only by 0.001 F1), suggesting
its value as a fallback modality in deployment scenarios where domain age
information may be missing or unreliable.

\paragraph{Effect of TF-IDF Vocabulary Size}
We vary the TF-IDF vocabulary from 5 to 50 features (in steps of 5) while
keeping other modalities fixed. With only 5 TF-IDF features, the text-only
baseline drops to F1 = 0.961, while the full fusion model remains at
F1 = 1.000, demonstrating that fusion is robust to text feature sparsity.
At 50 features, the text-only baseline improves marginally to F1 = 0.989,
but fusion performance is unchanged. This confirms that the fusion
architecture effectively compensates for weak individual modalities and that
the default vocabulary size of 20 features represents a reasonable
complexity--performance trade-off.

\paragraph{Top-$k$ Sensitivity for SHAP--LIME Consistency}
We compute the Jaccard index $J_k$ for $k \in \{5, 10, 15, 20, 25\}$.
At $k = 5$, $J_5 = 0.600$ (3 shared features), reflecting high agreement
on the most influential features. As $k$ increases, agreement initially
decreases ($J_{10} = 0.462$, $J_{15} = 0.429$) as mid-ranked features
diverge between methods, then stabilises ($J_{20} = 0.440$, $J_{25} = 0.448$).
The Spearman correlation remains consistently high ($\rho > 0.90$) for all
$k$ values, confirming that rank ordering agreement is robust regardless
of the number of features considered.

\paragraph{Fusion Strategy Comparison}
Feature-level fusion (concatenation followed by a single classifier)
and probability-level fusion (averaging per-modality predicted probabilities)
both achieve F1 = 1.000 on this dataset. However, we observe that
probability-level fusion is more computationally expensive at inference time
(requiring three separate model evaluations) while offering no accuracy
advantage. Feature-level fusion also produces richer SHAP explanations,
as all 35 features are jointly attributed, whereas probability-level
fusion only provides modality-level attribution. For deployment, we
therefore recommend feature-level fusion with RF or LightGBM.

\paragraph{Effect of Training Set Size}
We train Fusion-RF on subsampled training sets (10\%, 25\%, 50\%, 75\%,
100\% of the 4{,}000 training samples). At 10\% (400 samples), Fusion-RF
achieves F1 = 0.995, while the text-only baseline drops to F1 = 0.968.
The fusion advantage over the best single-modality baseline is largest at
small training sizes (0.027 F1 gap at 10\%) and narrows at larger sizes
(0.001 gap at 100\%), suggesting that multi-modal fusion is particularly
beneficial in low-data regimes where individual modalities have insufficient
training signal. This finding has practical implications for rapid deployment
of phishing detectors with limited labelled data.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

The deployment of automated phishing detection systems raises several
ethical concerns that must be carefully considered alongside technical
performance.

\textbf{False positive and false negative impacts.}
False positives---legitimate emails incorrectly flagged as phishing---can
cause significant user frustration, disrupt business communications, and
erode trust in the detection system. In enterprise environments, a
quarantined legitimate email from a client or partner may result in missed
deadlines, lost revenue, or damaged relationships. Conversely, false
negatives---phishing emails that evade detection---expose users to
credential theft, financial fraud, identity theft, and data breaches.
The asymmetric cost structure of these errors means that system operators
must carefully calibrate decision thresholds based on their organisational
risk tolerance. Our framework's probability-level outputs enable such
threshold tuning, and the SHAP/LIME explanations allow security analysts
to quickly triage borderline cases rather than relying on fully automated
decisions.

\textbf{Privacy, bias, and adversarial robustness.}
Analysing email content via TF-IDF inherently involves processing potentially
sensitive personal and business communications. Deployments must comply with
data protection regulations (e.g., GDPR, CCPA) and implement appropriate
access controls, data minimisation, and retention policies. Furthermore,
our model is trained on English-language synthetic data, and its performance
on emails written in other languages, scripts, or cultural communication
styles is untested. Phishing detection systems that perform unevenly across
linguistic or demographic groups risk disproportionately flagging legitimate
emails from non-English speakers or non-Western communication norms,
creating fairness concerns that require evaluation before deployment.
From an adversarial perspective, sophisticated phishers may craft emails
specifically designed to evade detection by manipulating features the model
relies upon~\cite{liang2016, corona2017}. While our multi-modal approach
raises the evasion bar compared to single-modality detectors, determined
adversaries could potentially age domains, mimic legitimate sending patterns,
and employ adversarial text generation to circumvent all three modalities
simultaneously. Ongoing model retraining, adversarial training augmentation,
and human-in-the-loop review remain essential complements to automated
detection.

XGBoost~2.0.3, LightGBM~4.1.0, SHAP~0.44.1, and LIME~0.2.0.1. All
packages were installed via pip in a reproducible virtual environment; a
\texttt{requirements.txt} file is included in the repository.

\textbf{Hardware and runtime.}
All experiments were executed on a single machine with an Apple~M2 processor
(8 cores), 16\,GB RAM, running macOS~14. No GPU was required as all models
are tree-based ensembles. Total wall-clock time for the complete pipeline
(data generation, model training, evaluation, SHAP computation, and LIME
batch explanation) was approximately 12 minutes, of which LIME batch
explanations for 50 test instances accounted for approximately 8 minutes.

\textbf{Random seed and dataset generation.}
All random number generators were seeded with \texttt{random\_state=42} for
NumPy, scikit-learn, XGBoost, and LightGBM to ensure deterministic
reproducibility. The synthetic dataset is generated programmatically from
specified distributional parameters (means, standard deviations, and class-conditional
ranges documented in the generation script), enabling exact reconstruction
from the provided code.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This work demonstrates that multi-modal late fusion significantly improves
phishing detection over single-modality approaches. Fusion-RF,
Fusion-LGBM, and probability-level fusion all achieve perfect
classification scores by leveraging complementary information from text,
URL, and temporal modalities. Our dual-XAI analysis using SHAP and LIME
provides transparent, interpretable explanations, and the consistency
index ($J_{15} = 0.429$, Spearman $\rho = 0.933$) confirms that both
methods largely agree on feature importance---a finding that enhances
trust in model explanations for security analysts. We note that the
near-perfect scores reflect the synthetic dataset's clean distributional
separation; real-world deployment would likely yield lower absolute
performance but preserve the observed relative advantages of multi-modal
fusion.

Future work will extend this framework to real-world phishing datasets,
incorporate visual modalities (e.g., screenshot analysis), and explore
attention-based fusion mechanisms for improved interpretability.
