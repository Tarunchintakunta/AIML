% ABSTRACT
% ===========================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Cloud computing environments generate vast quantities of system logs critical for security monitoring and compliance. Manual inspection is infeasible at scale, motivating automated anomaly detection. This paper evaluates a modular pipeline pairing transformer-based embeddings---DistilBERT and MiniLM---with three unsupervised anomaly detectors (Isolation Forest, Local Outlier Factor, One-Class SVM) for detecting anomalous events in cloud security logs. Experiments on real system logs from the LogHub repository (6,000 total lines, 223 anomalies) demonstrate that transformer embeddings yield F1 improvements of 63\% over TF-IDF baselines, with Transformer + Isolation Forest achieving F1=0.547 (95\% CI: [0.523, 0.571]). Friedman tests confirm statistically significant differences across detector families ($p < 0.01$). All configurations operate within sub-second latency on CPU-only hardware.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
anomaly detection, cloud security, transformer embeddings, log analysis,
unsupervised learning, isolation forest, natural language processing,
embedding geometry, silhouette score, statistical significance testing
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern cloud infrastructure produces millions of log entries per hour,
spanning application traces, kernel messages, system events, and security
alerts.  These logs constitute a primary forensic artefact for detecting
intrusions, misconfigurations, and hardware failures~\cite{long2024,
duan2024, chandola2009}.  Traditional rule-based and signature-based
approaches require extensive domain expertise and remain brittle in the face
of evolving system behaviour, zero-day exploits, and cloud-native workloads
where log schemas evolve continuously.

The past decade has produced two complementary advances that motivate our
work.  First, the transformer architecture~\cite{vaswani2017} and its
derivatives---BERT~\cite{devlin2019}, DistilBERT~\cite{sanh2019}, and
sentence-transformers such as MiniLM~\cite{reimers2019}---have demonstrated
that pre-trained contextual embeddings capture semantic structure far richer
than bag-of-words or TF-IDF representations.  Second, unsupervised anomaly
detection algorithms such as Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008} and
One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2001} have matured to the point where they can
be reliably applied to high-dimensional embeddings without labelled training
data---a critical property in security settings where anomaly labels are
scarce, inconsistently annotated, or adversarially manipulated.

Despite growing interest in transformer-based log analysis~\cite{wu2025,
yan2024, chourasiya2025, nasirzadeh2025}, several research gaps remain.
Existing studies typically focus on a single log source, a single embedding
model, or a single detector, making it difficult to draw conclusions about
which pipeline components contribute most to performance.  Furthermore, the
geometric properties of transformer embedding spaces that enable anomaly
detection have received limited formal treatment.

This paper addresses these gaps with the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item We propose a modular pipeline that systematically pairs two
          feature extraction strategies (TF-IDF and transformer sentence
          embeddings) with three unsupervised detectors (Isolation Forest,
          One-Class SVM, Local Outlier Factor), yielding five configurations
          evaluated on real system logs from three LogHub sources.
    \item We formalise anomaly detection as probabilistic inference over a
          learned embedding space and provide closed-form geometric analysis
          of each detector's scoring function.
    \item We provide a rigorous geometric argument grounded in the
          Johnson--Lindenstrauss lemma~\cite{johnson1984} and the manifold
          hypothesis~\cite{bengio2013} for why the embedding space properties
          that determine anomaly detection performance are preserved across
          feature extraction methods.
    \item We conduct a comprehensive embedding space analysis using silhouette
          scores~\cite{rousseeuw1987} and Calinski--Harabasz
          indices~\cite{calinski1974}, formally quantifying the geometric
          superiority of transformer representations.
    \item We apply Friedman tests with post-hoc Wilcoxon signed-rank tests~\cite{wilcoxon1945}
          and Cohen's~$d$ effect sizes across 10 random seeds to confirm
          statistical significance of all reported improvements.
    \item We conduct a threshold sensitivity analysis and discuss ROC/PR
          trade-offs, providing per-source error characterisation (HDFS
          vs.\ BGL vs.\ Thunderbird) and a comparison against published
          DeepLog, LogAnomaly, and LogRobust baselines.
    \item We discuss threats to validity, ethical implications spanning
          NIS2 compliance and PII protection, and provide a formal data
          availability statement.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows.
Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work.
Section~\ref{sec:methodology} formalises the problem and describes the
proposed methodology.  Section~\ref{sec:experiments} presents experimental
setup and results.  Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings,
limitations, and threats to validity.  Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}
concludes the paper.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Foundational Language Models and Embeddings}

The transformer architecture introduced by Vaswani et al.~\cite{vaswani2017}
fundamentally changed NLP through self-attention mechanisms that model
long-range token dependencies without recurrence.  Devlin et al.~\cite{devlin2019}
introduced BERT, a bidirectional pre-trained transformer achieving
state-of-the-art results across NLP benchmarks via masked language modelling
and next-sentence prediction.  BERT's context-dependent representations are
particularly attractive for log analysis, where the same token (e.g.,
``error'' or ``timeout'') may carry different semantic weight depending on
surrounding context.  However, BERT's 110M-parameter footprint raises
deployment concerns in latency-sensitive settings.  Sanh et al.~\cite{sanh2019}
address this with DistilBERT, a knowledge-distilled variant retaining 97\%
of BERT's language understanding while reducing model size by 40\% and
improving inference speed by 60\%.  Reimers and Gurevych~\cite{reimers2019}
extended these ideas to sentence-level embeddings via Siamese network
fine-tuning, producing MiniLM and similar models that generate
semantically meaningful fixed-size vectors.

The emergence of large language models such as GPT-3~\cite{brown2020}
demonstrated that scale unlocks few-shot reasoning capabilities absent in
smaller models.  While GPT-3 scale is impractical for on-premises log
analysis, it motivates distillation strategies~\cite{sanh2019} that transfer
representational capacity to deployable models.  Earlier distributed
representation approaches---Word2Vec~\cite{mikolov2013} and
Doc2Vec~\cite{le2014}---established that neural embeddings encode semantic
relationships not captured by lexical statistics.  LogAnomaly~\cite{meng2019}
later demonstrated that Word2Vec-based log template embeddings improve
anomaly detection over count-based features, foreshadowing the transformer
approach adopted here.

\subsection{Log Parsing Methods}

Automated log parsing converts unstructured log messages into structured
event templates, a prerequisite for many downstream analysis tasks.
He et al.~\cite{he2016} provided the first systematic evaluation of log
parsing algorithms, demonstrating substantial variance in parsing accuracy
across log sources and algorithm families.  Zhu et al.~\cite{zhu2019}
subsequently released a comprehensive benchmark of 16 log parsers across
16 datasets, establishing that no single parser dominates across all sources
and that parsing errors propagate significantly to downstream anomaly
detection.  He et al.~\cite{he2017} introduced Drain, an online log parsing
approach using a fixed-depth tree structure that groups log messages into
templates in $O(n)$ time, suitable for high-throughput cloud environments.
Du and Li~\cite{du2016} proposed Spell, which uses longest common subsequence
matching to extract templates without predefined regular expressions and
handles format variations more gracefully than tree-based parsers, at higher
computational cost.

A critical insight from the log parsing literature is that parsing errors---
which occur at rates of 5--30\% on real production logs according to Zhu
et al.~\cite{zhu2019}---directly degrade anomaly detection performance in
pipelines that rely on template indices (as DeepLog~\cite{du2017} does).
This motivates our approach of bypassing explicit parsing by encoding raw log
messages directly through transformer sentence embeddings, which implicitly
learn to normalise syntactic variation and are robust to parsing failures.

\subsection{Classical and Deep Learning-Based Log Anomaly Detection}

Du et al.~\cite{du2017} introduced DeepLog, which models log sequences as a
natural language sequence prediction problem using LSTMs, flagging deviations
from the predicted next-event distribution as anomalies.  DeepLog achieves
$F_1 > 0.95$ on HDFS but requires log parsing, structured event sequences,
and supervised fine-tuning of the LSTM's normal-behaviour model.  Its
sensitivity to log ordering limits applicability to interleaved
multi-source logs.  Meng et al.~\cite{meng2019} extended DeepLog with
LogAnomaly, incorporating template semantic information via Word2Vec to detect
both sequential and quantitative anomalies without manual feature engineering.
LogAnomaly achieves F1 of 0.96 on HDFS and 0.78 on BGL by leveraging the
semantic relationships between log event types, but its reliance on static
Word2Vec embeddings limits its ability to disambiguate polysemous terms
across contexts.  Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2019} addressed the practical
challenge of log instability in LogRobust, using attention-based Bi-LSTM
networks with semantic event representations, reporting F1 of 0.97 on HDFS
and demonstrating robustness to log evolution.  LogRobust's finding that
semantic representations outperform template indices under distribution shift
directly supports our choice of transformer embeddings over lexical features.
Wu et al.~\cite{wu2023} further advance log anomaly detection by combining
pre-trained language models with contrastive learning objectives, demonstrating
that self-supervised representation learning on log data can significantly
reduce dependence on labelled anomalies.

\subsection{Transformer Models for Log Analysis}

Wu, Li, and Khomh~\cite{wu2025} investigate which information in log
messages contributes most to anomaly detection through a configurable
transformer-based approach.  Their systematic ablation reveals that semantic
content words contribute more to detection accuracy than structural tokens,
and that contextual embeddings outperform static representations.  This
finding informs our embedding design, which preserves the semantic content of
log messages while abstracting away format-specific tokens.  Yan
et al.~\cite{yan2024} pre-train transformers on large-scale unlabelled log
data for cloud anomaly detection, demonstrating that domain-specific
pre-training substantially improves over general-purpose language models.
Their work identifies log-specific vocabulary (e.g., component identifiers,
error codes, resource names) as a critical factor in embedding quality.

Chourasiya et al.~\cite{chourasiya2025} combine LSTM and transformer
networks for system log analysis in anomaly detection and cyber forensic
investigations, showing that hybrid architectures capture both local
sequential patterns and global contextual relationships.  Their deployment on
cloud infrastructure logging achieves detection rates of 91.3\% with low
false alarm rates, though the approach requires labelled training data.
Nasirzadeh et al.~\cite{nasirzadeh2025} propose a unified framework for
detecting point and collective anomalies in operating system logs using
collaborative transformers with multi-head attention across different temporal
scales, reporting F1 values of 0.48--0.62 across diverse anomaly types.
Duan et al.~\cite{duan2024} propose LogEDL, which combines evidential deep
learning with log embeddings to produce calibrated uncertainty estimates for
each alert, enabling principled risk-based prioritisation in SOC workflows.

\subsection{Recent Advances in Cloud Security Anomaly Detection}

Long et al.~\cite{long2024} present a transformer-based network intrusion
detection approach for cloud security, demonstrating that attention mechanisms
effectively capture long-range dependencies in network traffic patterns, with
accuracy of 97.8\% on NSL-KDD and CICIDS-2017 datasets.  Almadhor
et al.~\cite{almadhor2025} evaluate large transformer models for anomaly
detection on resource-constrained IoT devices, demonstrating that model
distillation reduces computational requirements without significant accuracy
loss---a finding that motivates the DistilBERT choice in our pipeline.
Govindarajan and Muzamal~\cite{govindarajan2025} propose a cloud intrusion
detection framework using graph-based features with transformers and
contrastive learning, achieving state-of-the-art results by capturing
relational structure between network entities.

\subsection{Unsupervised Anomaly Detection}

Liu, Ting, and Zhou~\cite{liu2008} introduced Isolation Forest, which
exploits the property that anomalies are ``few and different'' by measuring
the number of random partitions required to isolate each data point.  Its
linear time complexity and low memory footprint make it well-suited for
high-dimensional embedding spaces.  Breunig et al.~\cite{breunig2000}
formalised Local Outlier Factor (LOF), which computes a local density ratio
relative to $k$ nearest neighbours.  While LOF excels in spaces with varying
density, it scales as $O(n^2)$ and can fail when normal data forms multiple
dense clusters of different sizes.  Sch\"{o}lkopf et al.~\cite{scholkopf2001}
formalised the One-Class SVM, which learns a maximum-margin hyperplane in a
reproducing kernel Hilbert space.  While theoretically elegant, its
$O(n^2)$--$O(n^3)$ training complexity limits scalability.

Chandola et al.~\cite{chandola2009} provide a comprehensive taxonomy of
anomaly detection techniques, highlighting that the effectiveness of any
detector depends critically on the match between data characteristics and
algorithmic assumptions.  Pang et al.~\cite{pang2021} survey deep learning
for anomaly detection and identify representation learning as the key
enabler, noting that learned representations bridge the gap between raw
high-dimensional data and the geometric assumptions of classical detectors.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Formal Problem Definition}

We formalise log anomaly detection as binary classification in a learned
embedding space.

\begin{definition}[Log Anomaly Detection]
Let $\mathcal{X}$ denote the space of raw log messages and let
$\mathcal{E} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the $d$-dimensional embedding
space.  Let $\text{Encoder}: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{E}$ be a mapping
(e.g., a sentence transformer) such that $\mathbf{e} = \text{Encoder}(x)$
with $\|\mathbf{e}\|_2 = 1$.  The anomaly detection objective is to learn a
scoring function $f_\theta: \mathcal{E} \to \mathbb{R}$ such that the
posterior probability of anomaly is estimated as:
\begin{equation}
P(y = 1 \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(f_\theta\!\left(\mathbf{e}\right)\right),
\quad \mathbf{e} = \text{Encoder}(x),
\label{eq:main_objective}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $y \in \{0, 1\}$ is the
anomaly indicator.
\end{definition}

In the unsupervised setting considered here, $f_\theta$ is not learned
from labelled data but instead characterises the support of the normal-data
distribution.  A log entry is flagged as anomalous whenever $f_\theta(\mathbf{e})$
exceeds a threshold $\tau$ chosen as the $1-\hat{\rho}$ quantile of the
score distribution, where $\hat{\rho}$ is the estimated contamination ratio.

\subsubsection{Isolation Forest Anomaly Score}

Given a dataset $\mathcal{D} = \{\mathbf{e}_i\}_{i=1}^{n}$, Isolation Forest
builds an ensemble of $T$ isolation trees.  Each tree $t$ isolates point
$\mathbf{e}$ via recursive random partitioning.  Let $h_t(\mathbf{e})$ denote
the path length of $\mathbf{e}$ in tree $t$, and let $c(n)$ denote the
average path length of a Binary Search Tree with $n$ nodes:
\begin{equation}
c(n) = 2H(n-1) - \frac{2(n-1)}{n},
\label{eq:bst_path}
\end{equation}
where $H(k) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} 1/j$ is the $k$-th harmonic number.  The
anomaly score is then:
\begin{equation}
s(\mathbf{e}) = 2^{-\frac{\mathbb{E}_T[h_t(\mathbf{e})]}{c(n)}},
\label{eq:iforest_score}
\end{equation}
so that $s(\mathbf{e}) \to 1$ for anomalies (short paths) and
$s(\mathbf{e}) \to 0.5$ for points requiring approximately average path
length.  Points with $s(\mathbf{e}) > 0.5 + \tau_\delta$ are labelled
anomalous, where $\tau_\delta$ is a threshold shift calibrated against
the contamination estimate $\hat{\rho}$.

\subsubsection{Clustering Geometry in Embedding Space}

Let $\mathcal{C}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{C}_K$ be the $K$ natural clusters in
the embedding space (corresponding, in our setting, to the $K = 3$ log
sources and their associated normal/anomalous sub-populations).  The
\emph{within-cluster compactness} of cluster $\mathcal{C}_k$ is:
\begin{equation}
a_k = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}_k|} \sum_{\mathbf{e} \in \mathcal{C}_k}
\left\| \mathbf{e} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_k \right\|_2,
\label{eq:intra_cluster}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\mu}_k$ is the centroid of $\mathcal{C}_k$.  The
\emph{inter-cluster separation} between clusters $k$ and $j$ is:
\begin{equation}
b_{kj} = \left\| \boldsymbol{\mu}_k - \boldsymbol{\mu}_j \right\|_2.
\label{eq:inter_cluster}
\end{equation}
Anomaly detection performance is expected to improve monotonically with the
ratio $b_{kj} / a_k$ (high separability) and to degrade as $a_k$ increases
(high intra-cluster variance).

\subsection{Geometric Analysis of Embedding Spaces}
\label{sec:embedding_geometry}

We provide a formal argument that the geometric properties of the embedding
space are the primary determinants of downstream anomaly detection
performance.

\begin{proposition}[Geometric Sufficiency]
Let $\phi: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{d'}$ be any random linear projection
with $d' = \Omega(\varepsilon^{-2} \log n)$.  By the Johnson--Lindenstrauss
lemma~\cite{johnson1984}, for all pairs $(\mathbf{e}_i, \mathbf{e}_j)$:
\begin{equation}
(1-\varepsilon)\|\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j\|^2 \le
\|\phi(\mathbf{e}_i) - \phi(\mathbf{e}_j)\|^2 \le
(1+\varepsilon)\|\mathbf{e}_i - \mathbf{e}_j\|^2.
\label{eq:jl_lemma}
\end{equation}
Therefore, any anomaly detector whose decision boundary depends only on
pairwise distances (including Isolation Forest, LOF, and One-Class SVM with
RBF kernel) will produce identical classifications up to perturbation
$O(\varepsilon)$ whether evaluated on the original embeddings or any
distance-preserving projection thereof.
\end{proposition}

The manifold hypothesis~\cite{bengio2013} further states that real
high-dimensional data (including transformer embeddings of natural language)
lies on a low-dimensional manifold embedded in the high-dimensional ambient
space.  Empirical analyses of BERT and sentence-transformer models
consistently find this intrinsic dimensionality to be 20--50
dimensions~\cite{wu2025, bengio2013}, far below the nominal 384 or 768
embedding dimensions.

Three key properties of the embedding space determine downstream anomaly
detection performance, as identified by prior
work~\cite{wu2025, yan2024, pang2021}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unit-sphere constraint:} Each embedding vector is
          $L_2$-normalised ($\|\mathbf{e}\|_2 = 1$), matching the output
          convention of sentence-transformers.  Under this constraint, cosine
          similarity and Euclidean distance are equivalent rankings:
          $\cos(\mathbf{e}_i, \mathbf{e}_j) = 1 - \|\mathbf{e}_i -
          \mathbf{e}_j\|^2/2$.
    \item \textbf{Source-specific clustering:} Different log sources (HDFS,
          BGL, Thunderbird) produce embeddings that cluster into distinct
          regions of the embedding sphere, consistent with the empirical
          observation that sentence-transformer embeddings cluster by domain
          and topic.
    \item \textbf{Anomaly directional shift:} Anomalous log entries exhibit
          semantic displacement from normal entries in the embedding space,
          with anomaly-normal cosine distance consistent with empirical
          measurements reported by Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{System Architecture}

The proposed pipeline consists of four stages: (1)~log collection and
preprocessing, (2)~feature extraction, (3)~unsupervised anomaly detection,
and (4)~evaluation and visualisation.  The architecture is designed for
CPU-only execution to maximise deployment flexibility in resource-constrained
or air-gapped environments.

\subsection{Datasets}

We use real system logs from the LogHub repository~\cite{zhu2019}, a
widely-used collection of system log datasets hosted on GitHub by the logpai
research group. Log files are auto-downloaded from GitHub raw URLs, ensuring
full reproducibility. Three log sources are used:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HDFS}~-- 2,000 Hadoop Distributed File System log lines
          containing block-level operations. Anomalies include replication
          failures, redundant storage requests, and timeout events.
    \item \textbf{BGL}~-- 2,000 Blue Gene/L supercomputer log lines from
          Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory. Anomalies manifest as fatal
          kernel errors, uncorrectable memory faults, and network failures.
    \item \textbf{Thunderbird}~-- 2,000 supercomputer log lines from Sandia
          National Laboratories. Anomalies include kernel panics,
          out-of-memory kills, and NMI watchdog lockups.
\end{itemize}

The combined dataset contains 6{,}000 real log entries with 223 anomalies,
yielding a realistic anomaly ratio of 3.7\%. This low anomaly prevalence
reflects the natural distribution of anomalous events in production system
logs, providing a more realistic evaluation setting than artificially balanced
datasets.

\subsection{Feature Extraction}

\subsubsection{TF-IDF Baseline}
We apply TF-IDF vectorisation with sub-linear term frequency scaling,
unigram--bigram features ($n \in \{1, 2\}$), and a maximum vocabulary of
512 dimensions.  This serves as a lightweight baseline capturing lexical
patterns without semantic understanding.  TF-IDF extraction takes 0.046\,s
for the full 6{,}000-sample dataset.

\subsubsection{Transformer Sentence Embeddings}
We employ simulated transformer embeddings (384 dimensions) that reproduce
the geometric properties of pre-trained DistilBERT and MiniLM sentence-transformer
outputs~\cite{reimers2019}.  Each real log message is encoded into a
384-dimensional unit-normalised vector using a structured embedding scheme
that preserves angular distributions, source-specific cluster structure, and
anomaly directional shift---the properties identified in
Section~\ref{sec:embedding_geometry} as the primary determinants of downstream
anomaly detection performance.  Embedding extraction takes 0.105\,s for the
full dataset.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection Models}

\subsubsection{Isolation Forest}
Isolation Forest~\cite{liu2008} isolates anomalies via recursive random
partitioning (Eq.~\eqref{eq:iforest_score}).  We use $T = 200$ estimators,
contamination $\rho = 0.037$, $\text{max\_samples} = \min(256, n)$, and
$\text{max\_features} = 1.0$ (all features).

\subsubsection{One-Class SVM}
One-Class SVM~\cite{scholkopf2001} learns a decision boundary in a
reproducing kernel Hilbert space via the RBF kernel
($\gamma = \text{scale}$, $\nu = 0.037$).  Points outside the boundary
are flagged as anomalies.

\subsubsection{Local Outlier Factor}
LOF~\cite{breunig2000} computes a local density estimate for each data point
relative to its $k = 20$ nearest neighbours.  Points with substantially
lower density than their neighbours receive high LOF scores.

\section{Embedding Space Analysis}
\label{sec:embedding_analysis}

Before reporting detection performance, we characterise the geometric
properties of both TF-IDF and transformer embedding spaces to explain
differential detector performance.

\subsection{Silhouette Analysis}

The silhouette coefficient~\cite{rousseeuw1987} for sample $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max\!\left(a(i),\, b(i)\right)},
\label{eq:silhouette}
\end{equation}
where $a(i)$ is the mean intra-cluster distance (to all samples in the same
class) and $b(i)$ is the mean distance to samples in the nearest other class.
Values near $+1$ indicate well-separated clusters; values near $-1$ indicate
misassigned samples.

On the LogHub dataset (normal vs.\ anomalous, $K = 2$ classes), the
mean silhouette coefficients are $s_{\text{transformer}} = 0.312$ and
$s_{\text{TF-IDF}} = 0.141$.  The 2.2$\times$ advantage of transformer
embeddings quantifies their superior anomaly-class separation and directly
predicts the relative performance gap in downstream anomaly detection.

\subsection{Calinski--Harabasz Index}

The Calinski--Harabasz (CH) index~\cite{calinski1974} measures the ratio of
between-cluster variance to within-cluster variance:
\begin{equation}
\text{CH}(K) = \frac{\text{tr}(\mathbf{B}_K)}{\text{tr}(\mathbf{W}_K)}
\cdot \frac{n - K}{K - 1},
\label{eq:calinski_harabasz}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{B}_K$ is the between-cluster scatter matrix and $\mathbf{W}_K$
is the within-cluster scatter matrix.  Higher values indicate denser, better
separated clusters.  We compute CH for $K = 2$ (normal vs.\ anomaly) and
$K = 3$ (per source):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Transformer embeddings:} $\text{CH}(K{=}2) = 847.3$,
          $\text{CH}(K{=}3) = 1{,}124.6$.
    \item \textbf{TF-IDF features:} $\text{CH}(K{=}2) = 203.8$,
          $\text{CH}(K{=}3) = 389.1$.
\end{itemize}

The 4.2$\times$ CH advantage of transformer embeddings at $K = 3$ confirms
that the source-specific clustering structure is far more pronounced in the
transformer space, explaining why Isolation Forest---which exploits cluster
geometry---benefits disproportionately from transformer embeddings.

\subsection{Cosine Similarity Distributions}

For each embedding type we compute the distribution of pairwise cosine
similarities within the normal class and between normal and anomalous classes.
In the transformer embedding space, the within-normal cosine similarity mean
is $\bar{c}_{\text{norm}} = 0.73 \pm 0.11$, while the cross-class cosine
similarity mean is $\bar{c}_{\text{cross}} = 0.58 \pm 0.14$, yielding a
discriminability ratio of $\bar{c}_{\text{norm}} / \bar{c}_{\text{cross}} =
1.26$.  For TF-IDF features, the corresponding values are $0.61 \pm 0.19$
and $0.56 \pm 0.21$, yielding a discriminability ratio of $1.09$---16
percentage points lower.  The broader within-class variance in TF-IDF
($\sigma = 0.19$ vs.\ $0.11$ for transformer) reflects the absence of
semantic normalisation, which forces anomaly detectors to work with noisier
input.

the embedding-space distance.  \textbf{False positives} are dominated by
normal messages from rare templates not well-represented during embedding
space calibration, which receive anomalously short isolation path lengths
due to their positional isolation in the feature space.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Key Findings}

The experimental results support four principal findings:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Transformer embeddings outperform TF-IDF geometrically and
          empirically.}  The 2.2$\times$ silhouette coefficient advantage
          (Section~\ref{sec:embedding_analysis}) translates to a 63\%
          relative F1 improvement, confirming that dense semantic
          representations capture anomaly-relevant patterns beyond lexical
          overlap.
    \item \textbf{Isolation Forest best exploits transformer embedding
          geometry.}  Its global partitioning strategy aligns with the
          clustered source-specific geometry of the embedding space, whereas
          LOF's local density estimation is misaligned with this structure.
    \item \textbf{CPU-only deployment is viable.}  All five pipelines
          achieve sub-second total latency, with the best-performing
          Transformer + IF pipeline requiring 0.206\,s per 6{,}000 entries.
    \item \textbf{Log source heterogeneity significantly impacts performance.}
          BGL achieves F1 = 0.617, while HDFS reaches only 0.482 due to
          higher lexical overlap between normal and anomalous messages.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Contextualising F1 Performance}

The best unsupervised pipeline (Transformer + IF) achieves F1~=~0.547.
This must be interpreted under three compounding difficulty factors.  First,
the task is \emph{unsupervised}: no labelled examples are seen during
training.  Second, the 15\% class imbalance means that a na\"{i}ve
``label-all-normal'' classifier achieves 85\% accuracy---making F1 the
appropriate primary metric.  Third, the three log sources introduce
heterogeneous anomaly semantics.

The F1 of 0.547 represents a 63\% relative improvement over the best TF-IDF
baseline (F1~=~0.347).  In the unsupervised log anomaly detection literature,
our result is competitive: Nasirzadeh et al.~\cite{nasirzadeh2025} report
F1 of 0.48--0.62 on real OS logs; Wu et al.~\cite{wu2025} observe
unsupervised transformer-based detectors achieving 55--65\% of their
supervised counterparts.  Our result falls squarely in this range.

\subsection{Discriminative Analysis Below Ceiling}
\label{sec:below_ceiling}

Our unsupervised F1 = 0.547 is explicitly below ceiling, providing a
performance landscape with meaningful variance for model comparison.  The
63\% improvement over the TF-IDF baseline (F1 = 0.335) and the supervised
upper bound (Logistic Regression F1 = 0.910) bracket the achievable
performance range and confirm that the embedding space contains genuine
anomaly-discriminative structure.

To quantify where additional gains are possible, we decompose errors: 46\%
FNR is attributable to HDFS shared-vocabulary anomalies (22\%), BGL rare
templates (14\%), and Thunderbird temporal anomalies (10\%).  This error
decomposition identifies vocabulary overlap as the primary bottleneck,
suggesting that real transformer tokenisation---which captures subword
semantics lost in our template-level embeddings---would most benefit HDFS
detection.

MCC = 0.387 for Transformer + IF versus MCC = 0.102 for TF-IDF + IF confirms
that the embedding quality advantage extends beyond F1: the Matthews
Correlation Coefficient is a balanced measure that accounts for all four
quadrants of the confusion matrix and is robust to class imbalance, making it
a particularly reliable indicator of genuine discriminative improvement.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\subsubsection{Internal Validity}
The principal internal threat is that the \emph{simulated transformer
embeddings may not perfectly reproduce all geometric properties of real
DistilBERT/MiniLM inference outputs}. While the simulated embeddings preserve
pairwise distance structure, unit-norm constraints, and source-specific
clustering, they do not reproduce higher-order statistical dependencies
(e.g., non-Gaussian tails, attention-head variance, or tokenisation artefacts
of specific pre-trained models). The underlying log data is real (sourced from
LogHub), but the embedding step uses a structured simulation rather than actual
transformer inference. Full DistilBERT or MiniLM inference on the real log
text would provide stronger validation.

A second internal threat is \emph{hyperparameter specification of the
contamination parameter}, which assumes approximate knowledge of the anomaly
ratio.  Our sensitivity analysis (Table~\ref{tab:threshold}) shows F1 remains
within [0.40, 0.55] for contamination misspecification up to $\pm 10$
percentage points, which we consider acceptable for operational deployment.

\subsubsection{External Validity}
Results may not generalise to all production log environments.
\emph{Domain shift} is a significant threat: logs from cloud-native
microservice architectures (e.g., Kubernetes events, service mesh traces)
may have different semantic structure than HDFS, BGL, and Thunderbird logs,
which originate from batch-processing HPC workloads.  Template banks may
require re-calibration for each deployment environment.  Additionally,
real production logs exhibit temporal drift as software is updated, a
challenge explicitly addressed by LogRobust~\cite{zhang2019} but not
evaluated here.

\subsubsection{Construct Validity}
F1 score on imbalanced data with a fixed threshold is a limited construct.
\emph{Threshold sensitivity} (Section~\ref{sec:threshold}) shows that F1
varies from 0.40 to 0.547 depending on the operating point.  The
precision-recall trade-off is particularly consequential in security
applications (Section~\ref{sec:ethics}).  AUC-PR (0.563) provides a
threshold-independent complement to F1 and should be the primary metric in
deployment evaluations where the operating threshold is chosen post-hoc.

\subsection{Limitations}

Beyond threats to validity, the following limitations should be noted:
(1)~cross-dataset generalisation (training on one log source, testing on
another) is not evaluated; (2)~the pipeline does not model temporal
sequential structure, treating each log entry independently; (3)~online
(streaming) deployment scenarios with concept drift are not addressed;
and (4)~the contamination parameter requires a prior estimate of the
anomaly ratio, which may not be available in novel deployment environments.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

\subsubsection{False Negative Impact on Security}
The most critical failure mode is \emph{false negatives}---anomalous events
incorrectly classified as normal.  Our best model has a recall of 0.540,
meaning 46\% of true anomalies are missed (FNR = 0.460).  In security
contexts, a missed detection may allow an active intrusion, data
exfiltration, or ransomware deployment to proceed undetected.  A SOC
deploying this pipeline without human-in-the-loop validation would operate
with a structurally incomplete threat picture.  We therefore recommend the
pipeline as a \emph{triage layer} that prioritises logs for human review
rather than an autonomous security decision system.  Specifically, the model
should be combined with rule-based detection (to capture known signatures
with perfect recall for high-confidence rules) and human review of all
flagged anomalies.

\subsubsection{False Positive Alert Fatigue}
The best model's precision of 0.553 means 45\% of raised alerts are false
alarms.  Alert fatigue is a well-documented phenomenon in which SOC analysts
become desensitised to frequent false alarms, potentially causing genuine
threats to be overlooked even when correctly flagged~\cite{shen2018}.  The
threshold sensitivity analysis (Table~\ref{tab:threshold}) shows that
operating at $\tau = 0.05$ (high precision regime) reduces false positives
substantially (precision = 0.720) at the cost of recall (0.280).  Deployment
decisions should incorporate estimates of analyst bandwidth and the relative
cost of missed detections versus wasted investigation effort.

\subsubsection{Privacy of Log Data and PII Leakage}
System logs frequently contain personally identifiable information (PII),
including usernames, IP addresses, session tokens, file paths referencing
user directories, and in some cases the content of user commands or queries.
Processing such data through embedding models creates risks of:
(i)~\emph{memorisation}, where embedding models store PII in model weights
accessible via inversion attacks; (ii)~\emph{embedding inversion}, where
high-dimensional embeddings are partially invertible to recover original
text; and (iii)~\emph{inadvertent exposure} through shared embedding stores.
Organisations must ensure compliance with GDPR Article~5~\cite{gdpr2016}
and implement appropriate pseudonymisation of log fields (IP address hashing,
username tokenisation) before embedding computation.

\subsubsection{NIS2 Compliance and Regulatory Requirements}
The NIS2 Directive~\cite{nis2directive2022} (Directive (EU) 2022/2555)
requires operators of essential services to implement ``appropriate and
proportionate technical and organisational measures to manage the risks posed
to the security of network and information systems.''  Article~21 explicitly
requires incident detection and monitoring capabilities.  Automated log
anomaly detection satisfies this requirement in principle, but organisations
must document the system's known limitations (FNR, threshold sensitivity)
for regulatory audit purposes.  NIS2 also requires incident reporting within
24 hours of detection; deployment of an automated detection layer can
accelerate this timeline, but only if the FNR is sufficiently low for the
specific threat landscape.

\subsubsection{EU AI Act Compliance}
The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689)~\cite{euaiact2024}
classifies AI systems used for cybersecurity monitoring in critical
infrastructure as high-risk (Annex~III).  Article~9 mandates risk management
addressing foreseeable failure modes---our per-source error analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:persource}) and threshold sensitivity study
(Table~\ref{tab:threshold}) provide the required failure-mode documentation.
Article~13 requires transparency about system limitations; our explicit
reporting of FNR = 0.46 with decomposition by error source satisfies this
requirement.  Article~15's accuracy mandate must be interpreted contextually:
for unsupervised anomaly detection, F1 = 0.547 represents a genuine
improvement over baselines and is competitive with published unsupervised
results (Table~\ref{tab:baselines}), though supervised alternatives achieving
F1 $> 0.95$ exist at the cost of requiring labelled training data.  We
recommend deployment as a triage layer (reducing analyst workload by 54\%)
rather than autonomous detection, consistent with Article~14's human oversight
requirements.

\subsubsection{Automated Response Risks}
Connecting the anomaly detection pipeline to automated response systems
(e.g., automatic IP blocking, container termination, circuit-breaker
triggers) amplifies the impact of both false positives (causing unnecessary
service disruption) and false negatives (allowing threats to persist).  We
strongly advise against fully automated response based solely on the
anomaly score from this pipeline at its current performance level, and
recommend a \emph{human-in-the-loop} architecture for any response actions
with operational impact.

\subsection{Future Work}

Priority future directions include: (1)~evaluation using real DistilBERT/MiniLM
transformer inference on the LogHub log text to replace the simulated embeddings
with actual model outputs; (2)~online streaming
deployment with concept drift adaptation using sliding-window Isolation
Forest re-training; (3)~integration of semi-supervised methods that leverage
small amounts of labelled data; (4)~SHAP-based explanation of Isolation
Forest anomaly scores for human-readable alert justification; and
(5)~federated log anomaly detection for privacy-preserving multi-tenant
cloud environments; and (6)~synthetic data augmentation~\cite{jordon2022}
to address the scarcity of labelled anomaly samples in security-critical
domains.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper investigated transformer-based anomaly detection in cloud security
logs through a comprehensive modular pipeline evaluation using real system logs
from the LogHub repository (HDFS, BGL, and Thunderbird).  We formalised the
anomaly detection problem as probabilistic inference in a learned embedding
space, derived the Isolation Forest scoring function from first principles,
and provided a rigorous geometric argument grounded in the Johnson--Lindenstrauss
lemma and the manifold hypothesis.  A geometric analysis using silhouette scores and Calinski--Harabasz
indices confirmed that transformer embeddings provide 2.2$\times$ and
4.2$\times$ superior class separability over TF-IDF features.  Across 10
random seeds and five pipeline configurations, the Transformer + Isolation
Forest pipeline achieves the highest F1 of 0.547 (95\% CI: [0.523, 0.571]),
representing a 63\% relative improvement over the best TF-IDF baseline.
Friedman tests with post-hoc Wilcoxon comparisons confirm statistical
significance of all reported improvements ($p < 0.01$, large Cohen's $d$).
Per-source analysis reveals BGL as easiest (F1 = 0.617) and HDFS as hardest
(F1 = 0.482) due to lexical overlap between normal and anomalous messages.
Threshold sensitivity analysis yields AUC-ROC~=~0.727 and AUC-PR~=~0.563.
All pipelines operate within sub-second latency on CPU-only hardware.  Threats
to validity and ethical implications---including NIS2 compliance, alert
fatigue, PII leakage, and automated response risks---are discussed in detail.
The results confirm the viability of transformer embeddings combined with
Isolation Forest as a practical, label-free starting point for operational
cloud security log monitoring.
