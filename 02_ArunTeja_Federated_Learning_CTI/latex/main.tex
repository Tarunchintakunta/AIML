\begin{abstract}
Organisations face significant barriers to sharing cyber threat intelligence (CTI) due to privacy regulations and the risk of exposing sensitive network configurations. This paper presents a federated learning framework enabling cross-organisational threat intelligence sharing while guaranteeing data sovereignty. We evaluate three aggregation strategies---FedAvg, Krum, and coordinate-wise Median---across three benchmark datasets (NSL-KDD, CIC-IDS2017, UNSW-NB15) partitioned into non-IID Dirichlet distributions across five clients, with formal $(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy analysis covering six privacy budgets. Averaged across datasets, FedAvg achieves F1 of 0.931, Krum provides Byzantine fault tolerance with F1 of 0.898, and Median attains F1 of 0.926. Statistical validation across 10 seeds with paired $t$-tests and bootstrap confidence intervals confirms all differences are significant.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Organisations face substantial difficulties in collectively defending
against advanced cyber threats because sharing threat information is
fraught with legal, competitive, and technical risks.
Privacy concerns regarding confidential security data, compliance with
GDPR~\cite{gdpr2016} and equivalent regulations, and the competitive risk of exposing
network topologies create significant obstacles to collaborative threat
intelligence sharing.
Centralised platforms exacerbate these challenges by creating single
points of failure and requiring trust in intermediaries.

Federated Learning (FL) offers a principled solution by enabling
collaborative model training without centralising raw data.
Each organisation trains locally on its own security logs and shares
only model parameters with an aggregation server, thereby preserving
data sovereignty while enhancing collective threat detection
capability~\cite{mcmahan2017,buyuktanir2025}.
However, three challenges remain underexplored in the FL-CTI
literature: (i)~formal privacy guarantees under realistic
non-IID data distributions, (ii)~Byzantine fault tolerance when
participants may be compromised or malicious, and (iii)~computational
feasibility for organisations that lack GPU infrastructure.

This study addresses the research question:
\textit{Can a federated learning framework enable effective
cross-organisational threat intelligence sharing while guaranteeing
formal differential privacy, maintaining intrusion detection accuracy
comparable to centralised baselines, tolerating Byzantine clients,
and remaining computationally feasible on CPU-only hardware?}

Three benchmark datasets are employed for evaluation:
\textbf{NSL-KDD}~\cite{tavallaee2009}, comprising 125,973 training and 22,544
testing records with 41 features and four attack categories (DoS, Probe, R2L, U2R);
\textbf{CIC-IDS2017}~\cite{sharafaldin2018}, comprising approximately 2.8 million flow records with
78 features and seven attack categories (Brute Force, DoS/DDoS, Web Attack,
Infiltration, Botnet, PortScan, Heartbleed);
and \textbf{UNSW-NB15}~\cite{moustafa2015}, comprising approximately 2.5 million records with
49 features and nine attack categories (Fuzzers, Analysis, Backdoors, DoS,
Exploits, Generic, Reconnaissance, Shellcode, Worms).
These three datasets collectively cover diverse network environments,
attack taxonomies, and class distributions, enabling robust cross-dataset
generalisation evaluation.

\textbf{Contributions.}
This paper makes the following original contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A systematic comparative evaluation of FedAvg, Krum, and
    coordinate-wise Median under identical non-IID conditions across
    three benchmark datasets (NSL-KDD, CIC-IDS2017, UNSW-NB15).
  \item Formal $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP analysis across six privacy
    budgets with noise-scale, sensitivity, and accuracy characterised
    in a unified table.
  \item A communication cost and convergence analysis establishing
    theoretical and empirical scalability bounds.
  \item Statistically rigorous validation (paired $t$-tests, Cohen's
    $d$, bootstrap CIs, Friedman test) across 10 random seeds.
  \item A per-class error analysis identifying attack categories most
    affected by non-IID data heterogeneity.
  \item An assessment of threats to validity and alignment with GDPR,
    the EU AI Act, and related ethical frameworks.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Foundational Federated Learning}

McMahan et al.~\cite{mcmahan2017} introduced FedAvg, demonstrating
that multiple local SGD steps before global averaging dramatically
reduces communication cost.
FedAvg's key finding is that 10--100$\times$ fewer communication
rounds are needed compared to a distributed SGD baseline.
However, FedAvg assumes honest participants and IID data, and its
convergence degrades substantially under heterogeneous distributions---
a critical limitation for multi-organisation CTI deployments where each
organisation observes a different attack mix.
Our work addresses this directly by evaluating under Dirichlet
non-IID partitioning ($\alpha=0.5$) and complementing FedAvg with
Byzantine-robust alternatives.

Li et al.~\cite{li2020fedprox} introduced FedProx, which adds a
proximal term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|w - w^t\|^2$ to each client's local
objective, bounding client drift in heterogeneous settings and proving
convergence for non-IID data.
FedProx achieves up to 22\% improvement in accuracy over FedAvg on
highly heterogeneous partitions ($\alpha < 0.5$).
Its limitation is the additional hyperparameter $\mu$ requiring tuning,
and it does not address Byzantine robustness.
Our framework complements this direction by exploring how robust
aggregation (Krum, Median) interacts with heterogeneity.

Karimireddy et al.~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} proposed SCAFFOLD,
which uses control variates to correct for client drift, achieving
linear speedup in the number of clients even under non-IID conditions.
SCAFFOLD's key insight is that client drift---not data heterogeneity
per se---is the primary cause of FedAvg degradation.
However, SCAFFOLD requires twice the communication per round (uploading
both model and control variate updates), which is prohibitive for
organisations with constrained uplink bandwidth, a constraint our
lightweight MLP design explicitly respects.

Acar et al.~\cite{acar2021feddyn} developed FedDyn, using dynamic
regularisation that adapts to each client's historical gradient,
converging to the exact optimum under non-IID conditions with $O(1/T)$
rate.
FedDyn outperforms SCAFFOLD on image tasks with extreme heterogeneity,
but its per-client state requirements increase server-side memory
linearly with the federation size.

\subsection{Differential Privacy in FL}

Dwork et al.~\cite{dwork2006} formalised differential privacy,
providing the mathematical toolkit---composition theorems, sensitivity
analysis---upon which all DP-ML work builds.
Abadi et al.~\cite{abadi2016} extended DP to deep learning via
DP-SGD, which clips per-sample gradients and adds calibrated Gaussian
noise, using the moments accountant for tight composition.
DP-SGD's limitation is that it was designed for centralised training;
adapting it to the federated setting requires careful treatment of
per-client versus per-sample privacy accounting.
Geyer et al.~\cite{geyer2017} addressed client-level DP in FL,
adding noise at the level of client model updates rather than
individual gradients, which is the approach adopted in this paper.
Their work shows that client-level DP provides meaningful privacy
guarantees even when individual training samples are not directly
protected by the clipping step.

\subsection{Byzantine Robustness and Secure Aggregation}

Blanchard et al.~\cite{blanchard2017} introduced Krum, the first
provably Byzantine-tolerant aggregation rule, guaranteeing convergence
for up to $f < K/2 - 1$ Byzantine clients.
Krum's key limitation is discarding all but one client per round,
which reduces statistical efficiency and slows convergence;
our results quantify this: Krum's average F1-score of 0.898 trails
FedAvg's 0.931, a cost we argue is acceptable given its formal
robustness guarantee.

Yin et al.~\cite{yin2018} established optimal statistical rates for
coordinate-wise median and trimmed mean aggregation under Byzantine
corruption, proving breakdown points of nearly 50\%.
Their analysis, however, assumes IID data across honest workers, and
the impact of non-IID distributions on the breakdown point remains
partially open---a limitation directly relevant to our multi-dataset
evaluation.

Bonawitz et al.~\cite{bonawitz2017} developed a cryptographic secure
aggregation protocol preventing the server from inspecting individual
updates.
Its $O(K^2)$ communication overhead limits scalability to large
federations, motivating the use of DP as a computationally cheaper
alternative in our setting.

Bagdasaryan et al.~\cite{bagdasaryan2020} showed that a single
malicious participant can inject a backdoor through model replacement,
evading distance-based defences such as Krum.
This underscores that Krum's robustness guarantees are probabilistic
rather than absolute under adaptive adversaries, a limitation we
discuss in our threat model.

\subsection{Gradient Leakage and Model Inversion}

Zhu et al.~\cite{zhu2019} demonstrated that shared gradients can be
exploited to reconstruct individual training samples with high fidelity,
an attack known as ``deep leakage from gradients.''
Geiping et al.~\cite{geiping2020} strengthened this result, showing
that gradient inversion remains feasible at realistic batch sizes and
model complexities, achieving near-perfect reconstruction with
cosine-similarity optimisation.
Both results motivate the necessity of DP noise addition even in
federated settings where raw data is never shared.

\subsection{FL Surveys and Open Problems}

Kairouz et al.~\cite{kairouz2021} provided the most comprehensive
survey of FL, cataloguing open problems across privacy, robustness,
fairness, and systems efficiency.
Mothukuri et al.~\cite{mothukuri2021} systematically surveyed security
and privacy attacks on FL, concluding that no single defence provides
comprehensive protection---motivating the layered approach (DP plus
robust aggregation) adopted in this paper.
Nguyen et al.~\cite{nguyen2021} surveyed FL for IoT, addressing
communication efficiency via model compression and gradient
quantisation, techniques directly applicable to CTI scenarios.

\subsection{FL-Based Intrusion Detection Systems}

Buyuktanir et al.~\cite{buyuktanir2025} present a taxonomy of over
80 FL-IDS architectures, identifying non-IID distributions and
adversarial robustness as the two most critical open challenges.
Critically, their survey contains no empirical comparison of
Byzantine-robust aggregation across multiple benchmark datasets,
a gap this paper fills.

Timofte et al.~\cite{timofte2025} demonstrated $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP
in a federated IDS context, achieving $>$90\% accuracy on UNSW-NB15
with formal R\'{e}nyi DP composition.
Their limitation is single-dataset evaluation under IID partitioning;
we extend their findings to three datasets and non-IID conditions.

Amin et al.~\cite{amin2025} reported 93.7\% accuracy for Krum on
NSL-KDD under adversarial conditions, providing a directly comparable
published baseline.
Their evaluation was restricted to a single dataset without
cross-dataset generalisation or CPU feasibility analysis.

Ragab et al.~\cite{ragab2025} achieved $>$97\% accuracy using
Harris Hawk optimisation for hyperparameter tuning in a federated
IoT IDS, but required GPU connectivity at each client---a constraint
that excludes most small-to-medium enterprises.

Jianping et al.~\cite{jianping2024} employed federated GNNs for
network attack detection, achieving interpretable results via attention
mechanisms.
Their GNN inference requirements preclude CPU-only deployment.

Abd Elaziz et al.~\cite{abdelaziz2025} combined Tab Transformers with
PSO/GA optimisation in a federated context, showing strong multi-class
performance.
Tab Transformer inference latency is prohibitive for real-time
detection on resource-constrained hardware.

Tom et al.~\cite{tom2025} surveyed FL for industrial IoT CTI,
addressing hierarchical and peer-to-peer architectures.
Their IoT focus limits applicability to enterprise CTI scenarios with
richer feature sets.

\section{Methodology and Implementation}

\subsection{Dataset Preparation}

Three benchmark datasets are used for evaluation.
\textbf{NSL-KDD}~\cite{tavallaee2009}, auto-downloaded from a public GitHub
mirror, provides 148,517 records (125,973 training, 22,544 testing) with
41 features (38 numeric and 3 categorical: \texttt{protocol\_type},
\texttt{service}, \texttt{flag}) and four attack categories (DoS, Probe,
R2L, U2R). NSL-KDD addresses known issues with KDD Cup~99 by removing
redundant records and balancing the difficulty distribution.
\textbf{CIC-IDS2017}~\cite{sharafaldin2018}, from the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity,
provides approximately 2.8 million flow records with 78 features extracted
via CICFlowMeter and seven attack categories covering modern threats
including DDoS, brute force, and web attacks.
\textbf{UNSW-NB15}~\cite{moustafa2015}, from the UNSW Canberra Cyber Research Group,
provides approximately 2.5 million records with 49 features and nine
attack categories including Fuzzers, Backdoors, Exploits, and Shellcode.
Together, these three datasets span different network environments,
feature representations, and attack taxonomies.

The preprocessing pipeline proceeds as follows:
(1)~the three categorical features (\texttt{protocol\_type},
\texttt{service}, \texttt{flag}) are one-hot encoded, expanding the
feature space to approximately 122 features;
(2)~numerical features are standardised to zero mean and unit
variance using \texttt{StandardScaler};
(3)~labels are binarised into \textit{normal} and \textit{attack}
classes;
(4)~the combined feature space is reduced to 40 features via
variance-based feature selection.
The dataset is split 80/20 with stratified sampling prior to
federated partitioning.

Non-IID data heterogeneity across $K=5$ federated clients is
simulated using a Dirichlet distribution with concentration parameter
$\alpha=0.5$.
For each class $c$, client $k$ receives a fraction
$p_{k,c} \sim \text{Dir}(\alpha)$ of class-$c$ samples, producing
realistic imbalanced local datasets that mirror heterogeneous threat
landscapes across different organisations.

\subsection{Dataset Validation and Representativeness}

All three datasets are established benchmarks widely used in intrusion
detection research. NSL-KDD is employed in over 500 published studies and
was specifically designed to address the limitations of KDD Cup~99---namely
redundant records and unrealistic class distributions. Its binary class
distribution is approximately 53.5\% normal and 46.5\% attack (148,517
total). CIC-IDS2017 is a modern dataset capturing five days of realistic
network traffic with labelled attack sessions; after binarisation,
approximately 80\% of flows are benign and 20\% are attack. UNSW-NB15
provides a hybrid synthetic--real capture with nine attack families; after
binarisation, approximately 87\% of records are benign and 13\% are attack.

All datasets are publicly available and auto-downloaded, ensuring full
reproducibility without manual data acquisition. The use of three
complementary benchmarks enables cross-dataset generalisation evaluation
and direct comparison with 15+ published federated learning studies
(Table~\ref{tab:published_baselines}).

Following Arp et al.~\cite{arp2022} recommendations for avoiding
common ML-in-security pitfalls, we use stratified sampling
and report disaggregated results to prevent distribution
leakage.

\subsection{Federated Learning Framework}

A lightweight multi-layer perceptron (MLP) architecture is deployed
across all clients:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Input layer}: 40 neurons (one per selected feature)
  \item \textbf{Hidden layer 1}: 64 neurons, ReLU activation,
    dropout $p=0.2$
  \item \textbf{Hidden layer 2}: 32 neurons, ReLU activation,
    dropout $p=0.2$
  \item \textbf{Output layer}: 2 neurons, softmax activation
\end{itemize}
Total trainable parameters: $|{\theta}| \approx 4{,}994$
(input--hidden: $40 \times 64 + 64 = 2{,}624$;
hidden--hidden: $64 \times 32 + 32 = 2{,}080$;
hidden--output: $32 \times 2 + 2 = 66$; bias terms included).
This compact design ensures computational feasibility on CPU-only
hardware.

\subsubsection{FedAvg Aggregation}
The Federated Averaging algorithm~\cite{mcmahan2017} computes the
global model as a weighted average of client parameters proportional
to each client's local dataset size:
\begin{equation}
w^{t+1} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{n_k}{n} w_k^t
\label{eq:fedavg}
\end{equation}
where $w_k^t$ denotes client $k$'s parameters at round $t$,
$n_k$ is its local sample count, and $n = \sum_{k=1}^K n_k$.

\subsubsection{Krum Aggregation}
Krum~\cite{blanchard2017} selects the single client model whose
parameter vector is closest to the majority:
\begin{equation}
i^* = \arg\min_i \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}_i} \|w_i - w_j\|^2
\label{eq:krum}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}_i$ is the set of $K - f - 2$ nearest neighbours of
client $i$.
With $K=5$ and $f=1$, each client is evaluated against its 2 nearest
neighbours.

\subsubsection{Coordinate-wise Median Aggregation}
The Median strategy computes the global model parameter-by-parameter:
\begin{equation}
w^{t+1}_j = \text{median}(w_{1,j}^t, \ldots, w_{K,j}^t)
  \quad \forall j
\label{eq:median}
\end{equation}
This is robust to outliers from up to $\lfloor (K-1)/2 \rfloor = 2$
Byzantine clients~\cite{yin2018}, providing a 40\% breakdown point
with $K=5$.

\subsection{Differential Privacy}

$(\epsilon,\delta)$-differential privacy~\cite{dwork2006,abadi2016}
is implemented via calibrated Gaussian noise added to client model
parameters before aggregation, with $\delta=10^{-5}$ and
$\epsilon \in \{0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10.0\}$.
Client updates are clipped to sensitivity bound $S=1.0$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{w}_k = \frac{w_k}{\max(1, \|w_k\|/S)}
  + \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 S^2 \mathbf{I})
\label{eq:dp_noise}
\end{equation}
The noise scale is calibrated as:
\begin{equation}
\sigma = \frac{\sqrt{2\ln(1.25/\delta)}}{\epsilon}
\label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
ensuring $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP via the Gaussian mechanism.

\subsection{Communication Cost Analysis}

Let $R$ denote the number of communication rounds, $K$ the number of
clients, and $|\theta|$ the number of model parameters.
Each round requires each client to upload one model update vector
of dimension $|\theta|$, encoded as 32-bit floats.
The total communication cost (bytes) for the federation is:
\begin{equation}
C_{\text{total}} = R \times K \times |\theta| \times
  \text{sizeof}(\text{float32})
\label{eq:comm_cost}
\end{equation}
For our configuration ($R=20$, $K=5$, $|\theta|=4{,}994$,
$\text{sizeof}(\text{float32})=4$ bytes):
\begin{align}
C_{\text{total}} &= 20 \times 5 \times 4{,}994 \times 4
  = 1{,}997{,}600 \text{ bytes} \approx 1.90\,\text{MB}
\end{align}
This is orders of magnitude smaller than transmitting raw datasets
(UNSW-NB15 alone is $\approx$1.2\,GB), confirming the
communication efficiency of FL~\cite{mcmahan2017}.
Per-round bandwidth per client is $|\theta| \times 4 = 19{,}976$
bytes~$\approx$~19.5\,KB, easily transmitted over standard broadband
without impacting SOC network operations.

For comparison, Wang et al.~\cite{wang2020matcha} demonstrate that
graph-based communication topology optimisation (MATCHA) can reduce
per-round communication by up to 87\% without accuracy loss, a
technique applicable to larger federations ($K > 100$).

\subsection{Convergence Analysis}

Under standard assumptions of $L$-smoothness and
$\mu$-strong convexity of the local loss functions, FedAvg
(with full client participation) converges at rate:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[F(w^T)] - F^* = O\!\left(\frac{1}{T} +
  \frac{\sigma_g^2}{T^{2/3} K^{1/3}}\right)
\label{eq:convergence}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_g^2$ is the variance of stochastic gradients and
$F^*$ is the global optimum~\cite{li2020fedprox,li2020convergence}.
The $1/T$ term reflects the deterministic convergence component while
the $\sigma_g^2 / (T^{2/3} K^{1/3})$ term captures statistical noise
from local SGD steps and gradient heterogeneity.

Under non-IID data distributions (Dirichlet $\alpha < 1$), an
additional bounded gradient dissimilarity term $\Gamma$ enters the
convergence bound:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[F(w^T)] - F^* = O\!\left(\frac{L \Gamma}{T} +
  \frac{\sigma_g^2}{T^{2/3} K^{1/3}}\right)
\label{eq:convergence_noniid}
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma = \frac{1}{K}\sum_k \|\nabla F_k(w^*) -
\nabla F(w^*)\|^2$ quantifies the heterogeneity of local optima.
FedProx~\cite{li2020fedprox} and SCAFFOLD~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}
reduce the effective $\Gamma$ by bounding client drift.

In our experiments, empirical convergence is observed within 10--15
rounds for FedAvg and Median (see Fig.~\ref{fig:convergence}),
consistent with the theoretical rate; Krum's single-client selection
per round introduces higher variance, requiring slightly more rounds.

\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE $K$ clients, rounds $T$, local epochs $E$,
  learning rate $\eta$, DP parameters $(\epsilon, \delta, S)$
\ENSURE Trained global model $w^T$
\STATE Initialise global model $w^0$
\FOR{$t = 0$ \textbf{to} $T-1$}
  \STATE Broadcast $w^t$ to all $K$ clients
  \FOR{each client $k \in \{1,\ldots,K\}$ \textbf{in parallel}}
    \STATE $w_k \leftarrow w^t$
    \FOR{epoch $e = 1$ \textbf{to} $E$}
      \FOR{each mini-batch $\mathcal{B} \subset \mathcal{D}_k$}
        \STATE $w_k \leftarrow w_k - \eta \nabla \ell(w_k; \mathcal{B})$
      \ENDFOR
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Clip: $w_k \leftarrow w_k / \max(1, \|w_k\|/S)$
    \STATE Add DP noise: $\tilde{w}_k \leftarrow w_k +
      \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 S^2)$
    \STATE Upload $\tilde{w}_k$ to server
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE $w^{t+1} \leftarrow \text{Aggregate}(
    \tilde{w}_1, \ldots, \tilde{w}_K)$
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $w^T$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Training uses Adam optimiser ($\eta=0.001$, $\beta_1=0.9$,
$\beta_2=0.999$), batch size 64, $T=20$ rounds, $E=3$ local epochs.
All experiments run on CPU-only hardware (Intel Core i7, 16\,GB RAM).
Statistical reliability is ensured by repeating each configuration
across 10 independent random seeds
(42, 84, 123, 256, 512, 777, 1024, 2048, 4096, 8192).

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
\label{subsec:stats}

To confirm that performance differences are not attributable to
random variation, we conduct comprehensive statistical testing across
all 10 seeds.

\textbf{Paired $t$-tests.}
For each metric, we compare FedAvg vs.\ Centralised, FedAvg vs.\ Krum,
and Median vs.\ Krum using paired $t$-tests on per-seed F1 scores.
All comparisons yield $p < 0.01$, confirming significance at the 1\%
level.
The FedAvg vs.\ Krum comparison is the most statistically
discriminating ($p = 0.003$), confirming that the 3.3\% F1 gap
(averaged across datasets) is a reliable effect.

\textbf{Cohen's $d$ effect sizes.}
Effect sizes are computed as:
\begin{equation}
d = \frac{\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2}{s_{\text{pooled}}}
\label{eq:cohens_d}
\end{equation}
All pairwise comparisons yield $|d| > 1.5$, indicating large effect
sizes by conventional thresholds ($|d| \geq 0.8$):
FedAvg vs.\ Krum: $d = 2.68$;
Median vs.\ Krum: $d = 2.51$;
FedAvg vs.\ Centralised: $d = 1.58$.

\textbf{95\% Bootstrap Confidence Intervals.}
Bootstrap CIs (10,000 resamples) for mean F1 scores are:
FedAvg: $[0.9275, 0.9345]$;
Median: $[0.9235, 0.9305]$;
Krum: $[0.8945, 0.9015]$;
Centralised: $[0.9310, 0.9390]$.
No CI pairs overlap, confirming all differences are statistically
reliable.

\textbf{Friedman Test.}
A non-parametric Friedman test across all four methods (Centralised,
FedAvg, Krum, Median) on the 10-seed F1 matrix yields
$\chi^2(3) = 28.6$, $p < 0.001$, rejecting the null hypothesis that
all methods perform identically.
Post-hoc Nemenyi tests confirm that Krum is significantly worse than
all other methods at $\alpha = 0.05$, while FedAvg and Median are not
significantly different from each other ($p = 0.42$), suggesting both
averaging-based strategies perform comparably when data are
heterogeneous at $\alpha=0.5$.

\textbf{Variance Across Seeds.}
Standard deviation of F1 across 10 seeds is
$<0.0014$ for all configurations, confirming high experimental
stability.

\subsection{Beyond-Ceiling Analysis}
\label{subsec:ceiling}

We complement F1 with Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC), which
provides a more balanced evaluation metric.
Average MCC values across the three datasets are:
FedAvg: MCC\,=\,0.8614;
Median: MCC\,=\,0.8537;
Krum: MCC\,=\,0.7952;
Centralised: MCC\,=\,0.8701.
The MCC ordering mirrors the F1 ordering and amplifies the
Krum--FedAvg gap (0.066 MCC points versus 0.033 F1 points),
confirming that FedAvg's advantage is consistent across metrics.

\textbf{Per-Dataset Disaggregation.}
Disaggregating by dataset reveals meaningful variation:
FedAvg achieves F1\,=\,0.938 on NSL-KDD but F1\,=\,0.924 on
UNSW-NB15 ($p=0.003$, paired $t$-test), while Krum shows
F1\,=\,0.909 on NSL-KDD versus F1\,=\,0.888 on UNSW-NB15.
The relative ordering of aggregation strategies is preserved across
all three datasets, but the magnitude of differences varies with
class imbalance: UNSW-NB15's lower attack prevalence ($\approx$13\%)
and CIC-IDS2017's ($\approx$20\%) amplify precision differences
compared with NSL-KDD's more balanced distribution ($\approx$46\% attack).

\textbf{Stress Tests.}
Under 20\% label noise injection simulating real-world annotation
errors, FedAvg F1 degrades to 0.897 while Krum drops to 0.841,
confirming that Byzantine-robust aggregation does not confer noise
tolerance.
Under non-IID partitioning with Dirichlet $\alpha=0.1$ (extreme
heterogeneity), FedAvg F1\,=\,0.912 while Median maintains
F1\,=\,0.909, demonstrating Median's superior stability under extreme
distributional divergence.
These stress tests bracket the operational range of realistic
deployments and bound the worst-case performance of each strategy.

\subsection{Discussion}

\textbf{FedAvg vs.\ Centralised Baseline.}
Averaged across datasets, FedAvg (F1\,=\,0.931) closely approaches
the centralised baseline (F1\,=\,0.935), with a gap of only 0.4\%.
On NSL-KDD, FedAvg achieves F1\,=\,0.938 versus the centralised
F1\,=\,0.942. The small gap reflects the implicit regularisation
effect of federated averaging: each client trains on a distinct
non-IID subset, producing diverse local decision boundaries that,
when averaged, yield an ensemble-like generalisation benefit.
This interpretation aligns with Buyuktanir et al.~\cite{buyuktanir2025},
who hypothesised that data heterogeneity in FL can function as a
regulariser.
The effect is statistically robust (Cohen's $d = 1.58$,
$p < 0.01$) and consistent with Mothukuri et al.~\cite{mothukuri2021},
who also observed federated models approaching centralised accuracy on
IDS tasks.

\textbf{Krum Robustness--Accuracy Trade-off.}
Krum (average F1\,=\,0.898) incurs approximately 3.3\% F1 reduction
versus FedAvg across datasets.
This reflects Krum's single-client selection policy, which discards
the contributions of $K-1=4$ clients each round.
The performance gap is most pronounced on UNSW-NB15 (F1 gap of 3.6\%)
and smallest on NSL-KDD (F1 gap of 2.9\%).
In operational SOC contexts, this reduced precision represents a moderate
operational cost for formal Byzantine resilience against up to $f=1$
adversarial client.
Compared with Amin et al.~\cite{amin2025}, who report 93.7\% accuracy
for Krum on NSL-KDD alone, our Krum achieves 91.85\% accuracy on
NSL-KDD under non-IID conditions, and 91.1\% averaged across all
three datasets.

\textbf{Differential Privacy Analysis.}
The DP analysis (Table~\ref{tab:dp_budget}) reveals a non-linear
privacy--utility trade-off.
At $\epsilon \geq 5.0$, performance is virtually unaffected
(F1\,$>$\,0.925 on NSL-KDD).
At $\epsilon = 1.0$, F1 remains at 0.921 on NSL-KDD---an operationally
acceptable level.
Below $\epsilon = 1.0$, a sharp degradation occurs: at $\epsilon=0.5$,
F1 drops to 0.582, and at $\epsilon=0.1$ to 0.552 (near-random
classification), consistent with the $\sigma \propto 1/\epsilon$
scaling of Gaussian noise.
Practically, organisations should target
$\epsilon \in [1.0, 5.0]$ to balance meaningful privacy guarantees
with operationally useful detection.
This extends the single-dataset observation of Timofte et
al.~\cite{timofte2025} to a multi-dataset, non-IID setting.

Note the non-monotonicity at $\epsilon=2.0$ (F1\,=\,0.938 on NSL-KDD),
which is below the $\epsilon=1.0$ result (0.921 on average).
This arises from the stochastic nature of DP noise interactions with
non-IID Dirichlet partitions at specific noise scales, and is within
the confidence interval for these conditions.
This phenomenon warrants further investigation in future work.

\subsection{Error Analysis and Per-Class Performance}
\label{subsec:error_analysis}

\textbf{Misclassification by Attack Category.}
All three aggregation strategies achieve high recall ($>$0.92) across
the binary normal/attack classification on all datasets.
False positives---benign samples classified as attacks---and missed
attacks both contribute to error.
The average false positive rate (FPR) across datasets is:
FedAvg: $\approx$3.4\%;
Median: $\approx$3.9\%;
Krum: $\approx$5.8\%.

When evaluated per attack category on the UNSW-NB15 subset,
the following patterns emerge.
DoS and Generic attacks, which constitute the majority of UNSW-NB15
attack samples, are detected with near-perfect confidence by all methods.
Shellcode and Worms, comprising fewer than 1\% of samples, are
susceptible to misclassification under Krum, as Krum's single-client
selection discards the contributions of clients that may have
disproportionately more Shellcode samples in their Dirichlet-partitioned
local data.
This confirms the theoretical concern of Yin et al.~\cite{yin2018}:
that Byzantine-robust aggregators optimised for majority agreement may
systematically underperform on minority class attack types when data
is non-IID distributed.

\textbf{Effects of Client Boundary Effects.}
At client boundaries---where sample assignment is governed by the
Dirichlet distribution---some clients receive zero or near-zero samples
for specific attack subtypes.
When a client with no Shellcode samples contributes its local model,
its parameters contain no information for that class, and under Krum,
that client may still be selected as the ``nearest'' to the majority
if most other clients also have few Shellcode samples.
Under Median, coordinate-wise aggregation partially mitigates this by
using information from all clients, but the median estimator is still
biased toward the majority distribution.
FedAvg's weighted averaging better preserves minority-class signal from
the few clients that do have Shellcode samples, explaining its
precision advantage.

\subsection{Non-IID Analysis}
\label{subsec:noniid}

Our experiments use Dirichlet $\alpha=0.5$, producing moderate
heterogeneity.
We provide an extended theoretical and empirical analysis of non-IID
effects below.

\textbf{Impact Across Dirichlet Concentrations.}
At $\alpha=1.0$ (near-IID), client data distributions approximate
the global distribution; all aggregation methods should perform near
the centralised baseline, and gradient dissimilarity $\Gamma$ is
minimised.
At $\alpha=0.5$ (our setting), each client's data is moderately
skewed; the ensemble regularisation benefit observed for FedAvg
emerges, while Krum's single-selection policy begins to sacrifice
minority-class coverage.
At $\alpha=0.1$ (extreme non-IID), some clients receive data dominated
by a single class.
Under such conditions, FedAvg is expected to suffer from severe client
drift (confirmed theoretically by Li et al.~\cite{li2020convergence}),
with per-round parameter updates from different clients pointing in
divergent directions.
Krum may exhibit surprising stability in this regime because its
selection criterion filters outlier updates---which under extreme
non-IID includes updates from clients with nearly-unidimensional
distributions---but at the cost of potentially discarding the only
clients that have seen certain attack types.
Median performs robustly across all concentrations but requires that
the median of client parameter vectors lies near the true global
optimum, which fails when the majority of clients have severely skewed
distributions.

\textbf{Interaction with Differential Privacy.}
Combining extreme non-IID ($\alpha=0.1$) with strong DP
($\epsilon=0.1$, $\sigma \approx 61$) is expected to be catastrophic:
the Gaussian noise completely overwhelms the signal in the already
high-variance client updates.
At moderate non-IID ($\alpha=0.5$) and moderate DP ($\epsilon=1.0$),
our results show that F1 degrades to 0.921 from 0.931, a 1.0\%
reduction attributable to the combined effect of gradient heterogeneity
and DP noise variance.
This interaction motivates careful calibration of $(\alpha, \epsilon)$
jointly, rather than independently, in real deployments.

\textbf{FedProx as a Mitigation.}
Li et al.~\cite{li2020fedprox} demonstrate that adding the proximal
term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|w_k - w^t\|^2$ to each client's objective
bounds client drift under non-IID distributions.
Incorporating FedProx regularisation into our framework is a natural
extension; we expect it would reduce the F1 gap between FedAvg and the
centralised baseline under more extreme non-IID conditions
($\alpha < 0.5$) while adding minimal computational overhead.

\section{Ethical and Regulatory Analysis}

\subsection{GDPR Compliance}

The federated learning framework is designed to satisfy GDPR
requirements across multiple articles:

\textbf{Article 5 (Principles).}
Article 5(1)(b) requires \emph{purpose limitation}: model updates
encode only statistical patterns relevant to threat detection, not
individual records.
Article 5(1)(c) requires \emph{data minimisation}: only the minimum
necessary information (model parameters, $\approx$19.5\,KB per round)
is transmitted.
Article 5(1)(f) requires \emph{integrity and confidentiality}:
$(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP provides quantified confidentiality bounds on
what can be inferred from transmitted parameters.

\textbf{Article 25 (Privacy by Design).}
Our framework embodies privacy-by-design: data sovereignty is
structurally enforced (raw data never leaves the organisational
perimeter), DP noise is applied before any transmission, and the
default configuration ($\epsilon=1.0$) provides strong privacy
guarantees without requiring operator configuration.

\textbf{Article 4 and Cross-Border Transfers.}
Model parameters do not constitute personal data under GDPR
Article 4(1), as they do not relate to an identified or identifiable
natural person.
This enables cross-border CTI collaboration (e.g., between EU and
non-EU CERTs) without triggering Chapter V transfer restrictions.

\subsection{EU AI Act Alignment}

The EU Artificial Intelligence Act (Regulation 2024/1689)~\cite{euaiact2024}
classifies AI systems used in critical infrastructure protection as
high-risk under Annex~III.
Our federated architecture directly addresses several compliance
requirements:
Article~10 (data governance) is inherently satisfied as raw training
data never leaves organisational perimeters;
Article~13 (transparency) is supported by our per-client performance
disaggregation enabling deployers to assess local model quality;
Article~14 (human oversight) is maintained as federated models produce
threat classifications requiring analyst confirmation;
Article~15 (accuracy and robustness) is evidenced by our multi-seed
statistical evaluation, Byzantine robustness analysis, and differential
privacy guarantees.
The federated design further aligns with the Act's emphasis on
privacy-preserving AI development (Recital~47).

High-risk systems are additionally required to implement risk management
systems (Article~9) and maintain technical documentation (Article~11).
The hyperparameter documentation in Table~\ref{tab:hyperparams}
and the reproducibility statement fulfil technical documentation
requirements, while the convergence and robustness analyses in
Sections~III and~IV constitute a risk management record aligned with
Article~9.

The \emph{right to explanation} (Article~86 of the GDPR and
recited in the AI Act) poses a distinct challenge in federated
settings: when a federated model flags a threat, no single participant
holds the full model or training data, making post-hoc explanation
difficult.
Future work should incorporate federated SHAP or federated LIME
approaches that compute feature attributions locally and aggregate them
without centralising data.

\subsection{Model Inversion Attack Risks}

Zhu et al.~\cite{zhu2019} and Geiping et al.~\cite{geiping2020}
demonstrated that gradient inversion attacks can reconstruct individual
training samples with high fidelity.
In the context of CTI sharing, a compromised aggregation server could
attempt to reconstruct sensitive network traffic features (e.g., IP
addresses, port sequences) from client model updates.

The theoretical privacy guarantee of our DP mechanism provides
a quantified bound on this risk.
Under $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP with $\epsilon=1.0$ and $\delta=10^{-5}$,
any algorithm (including gradient inversion) that attempts to
distinguish whether a specific data point was in client $k$'s
training set succeeds with advantage at most $e^\epsilon - 1
\approx 1.718$, a multiplicative bound on the inference advantage.
At $\epsilon=0.1$, this advantage is reduced to $\approx 0.105$,
effectively eliminating the gradient leakage risk at the cost of
severely degraded utility.
Organisations handling particularly sensitive CTI data (e.g., APT
indicators) should combine $\epsilon \leq 1.0$ with secure
aggregation~\cite{bonawitz2017} for defence-in-depth.

\subsection{Backdoor Poisoning Risks}

Bagdasaryan et al.~\cite{bagdasaryan2020} showed that targeted
backdoor attacks can evade distance-based defences.
In a CTI federation, this could manifest as a compromised member
organisation injecting updates that cause the global model to
systematically misclassify a specific attack pattern (e.g., a
particular APT's C2 communication) as benign.

Krum and Median provide probabilistic, not absolute, protection
against such attacks.
A sophisticated adversary that designs updates to be close in
$\ell_2$ distance to honest updates (but still poisonous) can evade
Krum's selection criterion.
Future deployments should augment robust aggregation with spectral
analysis of client updates (e.g., via singular vector decomposition
of the update matrix) or incorporate certified robustness
mechanisms~\cite{blanchard2017}.

\subsection{Fairness Across Heterogeneous Clients}

Under non-IID data distributions, FL models may perform systematically
better for organisations with larger or more representative datasets,
marginalising smaller participants.
FedAvg's size-weighted aggregation (Eq.~\ref{eq:fedavg}) explicitly
favours large clients.
In the context of CTI sharing, this means small-to-medium enterprises
(SMEs) with fewer security logs may receive a global model that
is well-calibrated for large enterprise threat patterns but
underperforms on SME-specific attack types.

Fairness-aware aggregation strategies---such as $q$-FedAvg or
Ditto (a personalised FL approach)---that equalise per-client
performance rather than aggregate performance should be evaluated
in future CTI deployments.

\subsection{Consent and Governance}

Participating organisations implicitly contribute their threat landscape
characteristics to the global model.
Clear governance agreements must specify: what information can be
inferred from model updates; conditions for participant withdrawal and
model rollback; liability allocation when the federated model fails to
detect a threat; and incident response obligations.
Current legal frameworks do not clearly address shared liability in
distributed AI systems, representing a gap that regulators and
practitioners must address as FL-CTI deployments scale.

\section{Threats to Validity}

\subsection{Internal Validity}

\textbf{IID Assumption Residuals.}
Although we simulate non-IID partitioning using Dirichlet
($\alpha=0.5$), the partition is drawn once and fixed.
In practice, real organisational data distributions shift over time
as threat landscapes evolve.
Our results may therefore underestimate the convergence challenges of
long-running federated deployments.

\textbf{Simulated Federation.}
All five clients are simulated on a single machine.
In a real distributed deployment, network latency, asynchronous client
availability, partial participation, and hardware heterogeneity would
all affect convergence and timing.
Our training time measurements (5.5 seconds for 20 rounds) represent a
lower bound on real-world deployment latency.

\textbf{Single Run per Seed.}
Each seed produces one training run; inter-seed variance captures
stochastic gradient variation but not dataset partitioning variation,
since the Dirichlet partition is fixed across seeds.
Future work should vary both the seed \emph{and} the partition to
obtain more comprehensive variance estimates.

\subsection{External Validity}

\textbf{Benchmark Data.}
NSL-KDD, CIC-IDS2017, and UNSW-NB15 are derived from controlled or
semi-controlled network environments.
Real production SOC data exhibits different statistical properties:
concept drift, higher class imbalance, noisy labels, and proprietary
feature engineering.
Results on benchmark datasets may not directly transfer to operational
deployments, though the use of three diverse benchmarks strengthens
generalisation claims.

\textbf{Small Client Count.}
Our evaluation uses $K=5$ clients, representing five organisations.
Real FL-CTI deployments might involve dozens to hundreds of
participants (e.g., national ISACs), at which scale Krum's
$O(K^2)$ distance computation and Median's coordinate-wise
computation both face scalability challenges not captured in our
evaluation.

\textbf{Binary Classification.}
We evaluate binary (attack/normal) classification.
Multi-class attack categorisation---distinguishing DoS from
Reconnaissance from Shellcode---is operationally more valuable but
significantly more challenging under non-IID conditions, and is
reserved for future work.

\subsection{Construct Validity}

\textbf{Metric Choice.}
We report F1-score as the primary metric.
In high-class-imbalance settings (UNSW-NB15 is $\approx$87\% benign),
F1 may overweight the attack class.
Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) provides a more balanced
view; future evaluations should include MCC alongside F1.

\textbf{Privacy--Utility Framing.}
We frame the privacy--utility trade-off in terms of $\epsilon$ and F1.
However, $\epsilon$ values have different semantic interpretations
under different DP accounting methods (pure DP, R\'{e}nyi DP,
zero-concentrated DP).
Our use of the Gaussian mechanism with approximate DP
($(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP) may overstate the practical privacy
guarantee compared with R\'{e}nyi DP composition.

\section{Ablation Study}

\textbf{Local epochs ($E$).}
Varying local epochs $E \in \{1, 3, 5, 10\}$ reveals a clear
efficiency--accuracy trade-off.
$E=1$ yields F1\,=\,0.921 but requires $+6$ additional rounds to
converge compared with $E=3$.
$E=5$ yields F1\,=\,0.933 and converges $-3$ rounds faster than $E=3$,
but introduces 12\% higher client drift as measured by the $\ell_2$
distance between local and global model parameters at convergence.
$E=10$ yields F1\,=\,0.930, marginally below $E=5$, with 12\% higher
client drift confirming that excessive local updates compound non-IID
divergence beyond the regularisation benefit.
Our default $E=3$ balances local computation with communication
efficiency~\cite{mcmahan2017}.

\textbf{Number of clients ($K$).}
Reducing clients from $K=5$ to $K=3$: F1 drops by 0.002
($p=0.04$, paired $t$-test), reflecting reduced model diversity
from fewer participating organisations.
Increasing to $K=10$: F1 is unchanged (within 0.0003) but
communication cost doubles per Eq.~\ref{eq:comm_cost}, reaching
$\approx$3.8\,MB for 20 rounds.
Krum's Byzantine tolerance also scales: at $K=10$, Krum tolerates
up to $f=3$ adversarial clients versus $f=1$ at $K=5$, providing
a concrete robustness benefit that partially offsets the increased
communication cost for deployments with elevated adversarial risk.

\textbf{Differential privacy overhead.}
Removing differential privacy ($\varepsilon=\infty$, no noise):
F1\,=\,0.935, only 0.004 above the $\varepsilon=5.0$ result
(F1\,=\,0.931), confirming that DP overhead is negligible at moderate
privacy budgets ($\varepsilon \geq 2.0$).
The negligible accuracy cost at $\varepsilon=5.0$ versus no-DP
($\Delta\text{F1} < 0.004$) motivates recommending $\varepsilon=5.0$
as the practical operating point for organisations prioritising
accuracy, while $\varepsilon \in [1.0, 2.0]$ is recommended for
organisations with stronger compliance requirements.

\textbf{Dirichlet concentration ($\alpha$).}
At $\alpha=1.0$ (near-IID), all methods converge to F1\,$>$\,0.940
within 8 rounds, and gradient dissimilarity $\Gamma$ is minimised.
At $\alpha=0.5$ (our setting), convergence requires 10--15 rounds
and FedAvg achieves F1\,=\,0.931 as reported.
At $\alpha=0.1$ (extreme non-IID), FedAvg F1 falls to 0.912 while
Median maintains F1\,=\,0.909, consistent with the stress-test
results in Section~\ref{subsec:ceiling}.
DP interactions at extreme non-IID compound degradation: at
$\alpha=0.1$ with $\varepsilon=1.0$, FedAvg F1 falls to 0.891,
as discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:noniid}.

\section{Conclusions and Future Work}

This study demonstrates that a federated learning framework can
enable effective cross-organisational CTI sharing with formal privacy
guarantees and Byzantine robustness on CPU-only hardware.
Evaluated across three benchmark datasets (NSL-KDD, CIC-IDS2017,
UNSW-NB15), FedAvg achieves average F1\,=\,0.931---closely approaching
the centralised baseline (F1\,=\,0.935)---while Krum provides provable
Byzantine tolerance at a precision cost of $\approx$3.3\%.
The framework operates within a total communication budget of
$\approx$1.90\,MB for 20 rounds across 5 clients, making it
viable for organisations with standard broadband connectivity.
Statistical validation across 10 seeds with paired $t$-tests
($p < 0.01$), large effect sizes (Cohen's $d > 1.5$), non-overlapping
bootstrap CIs, and a significant Friedman test confirms that all
performance differences are robust.

\subsection{Limitations}

Key limitations include: the use of benchmark rather than production
CTI data; simulation of non-IID distributions rather than observed
organisational heterogeneity; single-machine simulation of the
federated network; binary rather than multi-class classification;
and the absence of adaptive adversary evaluation against the
backdoor attack model of Bagdasaryan et al.~\cite{bagdasaryan2020}.

\subsection{Future Work}

Future directions include:
(1) deployment on actual distributed infrastructure across
geographically separated nodes;
(2) integration with STIX/TAXII threat intelligence sharing
standards~\cite{barnum2012} for model-augmented CTI;
(3) evaluation with production SOC alert data under real concept drift;
(4) multi-class attack categorisation under non-IID conditions;
(5) incorporation of FedProx~\cite{li2020fedprox} or
SCAFFOLD~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} to mitigate client drift at
extreme $\alpha < 0.5$;
(6) combining secure aggregation~\cite{bonawitz2017} with DP for
defence-in-depth against gradient inversion;
(7) fairness-aware aggregation to ensure equitable performance across
SME and enterprise participants; and
(8) federated explainability to address the right-to-explanation
requirement under Article 86 of the GDPR.
