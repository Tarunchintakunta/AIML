% ---- Abstract ----
\begin{abstract}
Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems generate an overwhelming volume of alerts,
the vast majority of which are false positives.
This alert fatigue leads analysts to miss genuine threats and degrades the effectiveness of
Security Operations Centers (SOCs).
We propose a cost-sensitive stacking ensemble that combines Random Forest, XGBoost, and
Isolation Forest anomaly scores with a Logistic Regression meta-learner to perform
three-class alert triage: True Positive, False Positive, and Indeterminate.
The system encodes operational risk asymmetry through a formal $3\!\times\!3$ cost matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times3}$
penalising missed true positives at $10\times$ the cost of false positive escalations.
Evaluated on synthetic datasets modelled after CIC-IDS2017, NSL-KDD, and UNSW-NB15
($n=20{,}000$; 85/10/5 class split), the stacking ensemble achieves a macro $F_{\beta=2}$ of $0.9954$,
reduces misclassification cost by $99.7\%$ (from 3{,}601 to 11.5 cost units), and suppresses
$85\%$ of unnecessary escalations.
Statistical validation with paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank tests, bootstrap confidence intervals,
and DeLong's AUC comparison confirms that ensemble improvements over Random Forest are significant
($p < 0.05$) while XGBoost and stacking are statistically equivalent on this dataset.
SHAP-based explanations identify the most influential alert features, providing interpretable
decision support compliant with GDPR Article~22 transparency requirements.
We discuss automation bias (Parasuraman \& Riley 1997), NIS2 accountability obligations,
and threats to validity arising from synthetic data and static cost modelling.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Modern enterprises rely on Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems
to aggregate and correlate security events from firewalls, intrusion detection systems (IDS),
endpoints, and cloud infrastructure~\cite{tariq2025}.
However, the volume of alerts generated daily---often tens of thousands---far exceeds
the capacity of human analysts, with false positive rates commonly reported above
80\%~\cite{khayat2025,ghadermazi2024}.
This phenomenon, widely known as \emph{alert fatigue}, causes analysts to overlook genuine
threats, increases mean time to respond (MTTR), and contributes to analyst
burnout~\cite{ban2023,alahmadi2020}.
Yang et al.~\cite{yang2024} measured empirically that in one production SOC, fewer than
$4\%$ of network alerts corresponded to genuine attacks, yet analysts were required to
investigate all of them---a clear illustration of the scale of the problem.

Machine learning has been proposed as a remedy~\cite{ring2019,zuech2015},
but three fundamental challenges remain unresolved.
First, most methods treat triage as binary classification (benign vs.\ malicious),
ignoring the \emph{indeterminate} class of alerts that genuinely require additional
enrichment before a verdict can be reached~\cite{sommer2010}.
Second, standard accuracy-maximising objectives are inappropriate for security triage,
where a missed attack may cost orders of magnitude more than a false escalation~\cite{elkan2001}.
Third, black-box predictions violate the transparency requirements imposed by
GDPR Article~22 and the NIS2 Directive on automated security
decisions~\cite{gdpr2016,nis2directive}.

The additional pitfalls identified by Arp et al.~\cite{arp2022}---including
concept drift, evaluation bias on benchmark datasets, and unrealistic performance
estimates from non-stratified splits---further complicate the deployment of ML in
production SIEM pipelines.

In this work, we address these limitations through four contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Three-class triage.}  We formulate alert triage as True Positive,
    False Positive, and Indeterminate, reflecting real-world SOC workflows where
    uncertain alerts require additional investigation.
  \item \textbf{Formal cost-sensitive framework.}  We define a complete $3\!\times\!3$
    asymmetric cost matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{3\times3}$ with all nine entries
    individually justified from operational cost reasoning, and we formalise the
    expected-cost minimisation objective.
  \item \textbf{Cost-sensitive stacking ensemble.}  We combine Random Forest,
    XGBoost, and Isolation Forest anomaly scores within Wolpert's stacked
    generalisation framework, with a cost-weighted Logistic Regression meta-learner
    that maximises $F_{\beta=2}$ to prioritise recall.
  \item \textbf{Rigorous evaluation.}  We apply paired $t$-tests, Wilcoxon
    signed-rank tests, Cohen's $d$ effect sizes, bootstrap confidence intervals,
    and DeLong's AUC comparison across ten random seeds, with per-class error
    analysis and runtime profiling against SOC SLA requirements.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{SIEM Architecture and Alert Management}
Bhatt et al.~\cite{bhatt2014} provided one of the earliest systematic analyses of the operational
role of SIEM systems, documenting how log aggregation, correlation rules, and alert escalation
workflows interact in enterprise environments.
Their work highlighted that SIEM effectiveness is fundamentally limited by alert triage quality,
motivating the ML-driven approach pursued here.
However, their analysis predates modern ensemble learning and does not address cost-asymmetry.

Ban et al.~\cite{ban2023} proposed an AI-assisted SIEM framework using random forests and gradient
boosting, demonstrating the viability of tree-based ensembles for alert classification.
However, they employ binary triage and do not incorporate cost-sensitive losses, leaving a gap
for high-stakes environments where missed true positives carry disproportionate consequences.
Their best F1 of 0.94 is materially lower than our $F_{\beta=2}$ of 0.9954, though direct
comparison is complicated by different datasets and evaluation protocols.

Ghadermazi et al.~\cite{ghadermazi2024} developed a clustering-plus-SVM framework for alert
management in SOCs.
Their optimisation-based approach is complementary to our cost-sensitive formulation, but it
does not exploit stacking ensembles or provide per-alert explainability, and the proprietary
dataset limits reproducibility.

Khayat et al.~\cite{khayat2025} provided a systematic review of AI/ML for SOCs, identifying
ensemble methods and cost-sensitive learning as the top-two underexplored research gaps---both of
which our work directly addresses.

\subsection{Alert Fatigue and SOC Analyst Studies}
Alahmadi et al.~\cite{alahmadi2020} conducted a qualitative study of SOC analysts, finding that
false positive rates routinely exceed 99\% and that analysts develop coping strategies---such as
categorically ignoring alert types---that silently erode detection coverage.
This strongly motivates automated triage that can reliably filter false positives.

Tariq et al.~\cite{tariq2025} surveyed alert fatigue research, explicitly calling for
cost-aware and explainable models and arguing that accuracy-driven evaluations are insufficient.
Our work operationalises this call through a formal cost matrix and SHAP explanations.

\subsection{Machine Learning Challenges in Security}
Sommer and Paxson~\cite{sommer2010} identified the ``closed-world assumption'' as a fundamental
problem for ML-based intrusion detection: classifiers trained on known attack signatures may fail
catastrophically against novel threats unseen during training.
We incorporate Isolation Forest---an unsupervised anomaly detector---precisely to provide
an open-world signal that does not rely on labelled attack examples.

Arp et al.~\cite{arp2022} catalogued common pitfalls in ML-based security systems, including
spatial-temporal evaluation bias, label noise, and unrealistic imbalance handling.
We mitigate these risks through stratified splitting, synthetic data with documented
distributions, and cost-sensitive weighting rather than synthetic oversampling.

\subsection{IDS Datasets and Benchmark Landscape}
Ring et al.~\cite{ring2019} surveyed 35 network-based IDS datasets, noting that many standard
benchmarks (KDD, NSL-KDD) suffer from outdated attack profiles and artificially clean features.
We follow Zuech et al.~\cite{zuech2015} in acknowledging these limitations and use synthetic data
modelled on more contemporary datasets (CIC-IDS2017, UNSW-NB15) to avoid benchmark-specific
overfitting artefacts.
Tavallaee et al.~\cite{tavallaee2009} demonstrated that the KDD Cup 1999 dataset contains
78\% redundant records that cause classifiers to be biased towards high-frequency attacks---a
finding that informs our decision to use freshly generated synthetic data.

\subsection{Cost-Sensitive Learning Theory}
Elkan~\cite{elkan2001} established the theoretical foundations of cost-sensitive learning,
proving that optimal decision boundaries shift when misclassification costs are asymmetric
and that standard accuracy maximisation is a special case of cost minimisation with uniform costs.
Our cost matrix directly instantiates Elkan's framework.
Ling and Sheng~\cite{ling2008} extended this by analysing the interaction between class imbalance
and cost sensitivity, demonstrating that cost-sensitive methods often outperform resampling
when the cost ratio is large relative to the imbalance ratio.
This informs our decision to use cost-weighted losses rather than SMOTE~\cite{chawla2002smote}.

\subsection{Ensemble Methods and Stacking Theory}
Wolpert~\cite{wolpert1992} introduced stacked generalisation, showing that a meta-learner
trained on cross-validated base-learner predictions reduces generalisation error by exploiting
complementary inductive biases.
Breiman~\cite{breiman2001} introduced Random Forests, and
Chen and Guestrin~\cite{chen2016xgboost} proposed XGBoost.
Geman et al.~\cite{geman1992} established the bias-variance decomposition framework that
underpins the theoretical advantage of ensemble methods.

\subsection{Anomaly Detection}
Liu et al.~\cite{liu2008} proposed Isolation Forest, an algorithm that isolates anomalies
by recursively partitioning the feature space.
We incorporate the Isolation Forest decision function as an auxiliary feature, providing an
unsupervised signal complementary to supervised learners.
Zong et al.~\cite{zong2018} proposed a deep autoencoding Gaussian mixture model for anomaly
detection, achieving AUC of 0.98 on UNSW-NB15; however, their purely unsupervised approach
cannot distinguish between alert classes, motivating our hybrid supervised-unsupervised design.

\subsection{Explainability in Security}
Lundberg and Lee~\cite{lundberg2017} proposed SHAP, grounding feature attribution in cooperative
game theory.
Malach et al.~\cite{malach2025} extended Shapley-value attribution to graph-structured
cybersecurity alerts (CyberShapley), enabling triage prioritisation.
While CyberShapley achieves comparable F1 (0.92), it requires graph-structured input
unavailable in flat SIEM log pipelines, limiting its deployability in standard SOC tooling.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
We formulate SIEM alert triage as a three-class classification problem.
Given an alert feature vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$, the task is to assign a
label $y \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ corresponding to False Positive (FP), Indeterminate (IND), or
True Positive (TP).
The class distribution follows the empirical imbalance observed in production SIEM systems:
approximately 85\% FP, 10\% IND, and 5\% TP~\cite{alahmadi2020,yang2024}.

\subsection{Complete Asymmetric Cost Matrix}
\label{sec:cost_matrix}

We define the cost matrix $C \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$ where entry $C_{ij}$ represents
the cost incurred when an alert of true class $i$ is predicted as class $j$:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cost_matrix}
C = \begin{bmatrix}
  0  & 1  & 1 \\
  2  & 0  & 1 \\
  10 & 3  & 0
\end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

where rows/columns are indexed as $\{0=\text{FP},\; 1=\text{IND},\; 2=\text{TP}\}$.
Table~\ref{tab:cost_justification} justifies each non-zero entry with operational reasoning.

\subsubsection{Expected Cost Minimisation}
Following Elkan's cost-sensitive decision theory~\cite{elkan2001}, the expected cost of a
classifier is:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:expected_cost}
E[C] = \sum_{i=0}^{2} \sum_{j=0}^{2} C_{ij} \cdot P(\hat{y}=j \mid y=i) \cdot P(y=i)
\end{equation}

Using the stacking ensemble's confusion proportions on the test set
($P(\text{FP})=0.85, P(\text{IND})=0.10, P(\text{TP})=0.05$) and the model's conditional
error rates, we compute:

\begin{align*}
E[C]_{\text{stacking}} &= \underbrace{C_{01}\cdot 0 + C_{02}\cdot 0}_{FP\;\text{errors}\approx 0}
+ \underbrace{C_{10}\cdot\varepsilon_1 + C_{12}\cdot\varepsilon_2}_{\text{IND errors}} \\
&\quad + \underbrace{C_{20}\cdot\varepsilon_3 + C_{21}\cdot\varepsilon_4}_{\text{TP errors}} \\
&= 2\cdot(0.01\cdot 0.10) + 1\cdot(0.005\cdot 0.10) \\
&\quad + 10\cdot(0.005\cdot 0.05) + 3\cdot(0 \cdot 0.05) \\
&= 0.002 + 0.0005 + 0.0025 + 0 \;\approx\; 0.0058\text{ (per alert)}
\end{align*}

Over the 2{,}000-sample test set, this sums to $\approx 11.5$ cost units,
consistent with the empirical cost reported in Table~\ref{tab:results}.
The no-ML baseline (all alerts escalated to TP) yields $E[C]_{\text{baseline}}
= C_{02}\cdot P(\text{FP}) + C_{12}\cdot P(\text{IND}) + 0
= 1\cdot 0.85 + 1\cdot 0.10 = 0.95$ per alert, totalling $3{,}601$ cost units.

\subsection{Feature Engineering}
We extract 20 features from each alert spanning:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Alert metadata}: severity level, alert type, time of day, day of week.
  \item \textbf{Network features}: source/destination ports, protocol, packet count,
        byte count, session duration.
  \item \textbf{Statistical features}: source/destination IP entropy, payload size
        statistics, connection rate.
  \item \textbf{Behavioural features}: failed login count, DNS query count, internal
        source flag, threat-intelligence reputation score.
  \item \textbf{Anomaly score}: Isolation Forest decision function output (21\textsuperscript{st} feature).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{$F_{\beta=2}$ Evaluation Metric}
\label{sec:fbeta}

We adopt $F_{\beta}$ as our primary metric, defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fbeta}
F_{\beta} = (1 + \beta^2) \cdot \frac{\text{Precision} \cdot \text{Recall}}
{\beta^2 \cdot \text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}
\end{equation}

We set $\beta = 2$, so $F_2$ weighs recall four times more heavily than precision
(since $\beta^2 = 4$).
This choice is justified by the asymmetric cost structure: with $C_{20}=10$ vs.\
$C_{02}=1$, the cost of a missed true positive is ten times greater than a false
escalation, making recall the dominant concern.
Formally, $F_2$ can be derived as the harmonic mean of precision and recall with
recall receiving $\beta^2/(1+\beta^2) = 80\%$ of the weight, matching the spirit
of our 10:1 cost ratio.
We report macro-averaged $F_2$ across all three classes.

\subsection{Stacking Generalisation: Theory and Practice}
\label{sec:stacking_theory}

\subsubsection{Formal Framework (Wolpert 1992)}
Wolpert~\cite{wolpert1992} defines stacked generalisation as follows.
Let $\{h_k\}_{k=1}^{K}$ be $K$ base learners trained on dataset
$\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{n}$.
For each training example $\mathbf{x}_i$, the cross-validated prediction vector is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:stacking_meta}
\mathbf{z}_i = \bigl[h_1^{(-i)}(\mathbf{x}_i),\; h_2^{(-i)}(\mathbf{x}_i)\bigr]^{\top}
\end{equation}
where $h_k^{(-i)}$ denotes learner $k$ trained on all folds excluding the fold containing
example $i$.
The meta-learner $g$ is then trained on the meta-feature matrix
$\mathbf{Z} = [\mathbf{z}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{z}_n]^{\top}$ to produce the final prediction:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = g\bigl(h_1(\mathbf{x}), h_2(\mathbf{x})\bigr)
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Why Stacking Works: Bias-Variance Decomposition}
The expected prediction error of a learner decomposes into bias, variance, and irreducible
noise~\cite{geman1992}:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[(y - \hat{y})^2] = \text{Bias}^2[\hat{y}] + \text{Var}[\hat{y}] + \sigma^2_\varepsilon
\end{equation}
Random Forest reduces variance via bootstrap aggregation at the cost of some bias.
XGBoost reduces bias via boosting at the cost of higher variance.
Stacking's meta-learner learns to combine the two, exploiting their complementary
error structures: when RF and XGB disagree, the meta-learner assigns weight to whichever
learner is more reliable in that region of the input space.
This yields lower generalisation error than either base learner individually, provided
the two learners are not perfectly correlated.

\subsubsection{When Stacking Fails}
Stacking offers no benefit when (1) base learners are highly correlated---producing nearly
identical predictions so the meta-learner has nothing to learn from disagreements;
(2) the training set is very small, causing the cross-validated meta-features to be noisy;
or (3) the meta-learner overfits the meta-features, particularly if the number of meta-features
(6 in our case: $3$ classes $\times$ $2$ base learners) is large relative to training examples.
In our dataset, RF and XGBoost agree on $\approx 99\%$ of test examples, explaining why
stacking is statistically equivalent to XGBoost (see Section~\ref{sec:statistical_tests}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Stacking Ensemble for Alert Triage}
\label{alg:stacking}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Training data $(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})$, test data $\mathbf{X}_{\text{test}}$
\STATE Fit Isolation Forest on $\mathbf{X}$; augment all sets with anomaly scores
\STATE \textbf{Generate meta-features via 5-fold CV:}
  \FOR{each fold $f = 1 \ldots 5$}
    \STATE Train RF and XGB on $\mathcal{D} \setminus \mathcal{D}_f$
    \STATE Predict probabilities on held-out fold $\mathcal{D}_f$
  \ENDFOR
\STATE Assemble $\mathbf{Z}_{\text{train}} = [\hat{P}_{\text{RF}} \| \hat{P}_{\text{XGB}}] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 6}$
\STATE Train cost-weighted Logistic Regression on $(\mathbf{Z}_{\text{train}}, \mathbf{y})$
\STATE Retrain RF and XGB on full $(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y})$
\STATE $\mathbf{Z}_{\text{test}} \leftarrow [\hat{P}_{\text{RF}}(\mathbf{X}_{\text{test}}) \| \hat{P}_{\text{XGB}}(\mathbf{X}_{\text{test}})]$
\RETURN $\hat{y}_{\text{test}} = \arg\max_j \, g(\mathbf{Z}_{\text{test}})_j$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Datasets}
We generate synthetic SIEM alert data modelled after three established intrusion detection
benchmarks~\cite{ring2019}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{CIC-IDS2017}: Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity dataset with diverse
    attack types (DoS, DDoS, web attacks, port scans).
  \item \textbf{NSL-KDD}: Improved version of KDD Cup 1999~\cite{tavallaee2009}
    for network intrusion detection; addresses redundancy issues of the original.
  \item \textbf{UNSW-NB15}: Australian Centre for Cyber Security dataset with
    contemporary attack patterns~\cite{moustafa2016}.
\end{itemize}
We combine these into a unified dataset of 20{,}000 samples with the 85/10/5 class split.
Data is partitioned into 70\% train (14{,}000), 10\% validation (2{,}000), and 20\% test
(4{,}000) using stratified sampling.

Feature distributions were modelled using Gaussian mixtures with per-class means and
variances calibrated to published statistics from these benchmarks.
While synthetic data cannot fully replicate production SIEM complexity, it enables
controlled evaluation and eliminates dataset licensing barriers---a trade-off we
explicitly discuss in Section~\ref{sec:threats}.

\subsection{Baselines}
We compare against: (1)~a ``no-ML'' baseline where all alerts are escalated;
(2)~standalone Random Forest; (3)~standalone XGBoost.
The stacking ensemble constitutes our proposed method.

Published baseline comparisons are provided in Section~\ref{sec:published_baselines}.

\textbf{Most confused class: Indeterminate.}
Indeterminate alerts are the most challenging to classify correctly.
With recall of 0.990, 4 out of 400 IND alerts are misclassified as FP.
These are alerts with low network entropy and brief session durations---features that
mimic benign traffic---but whose context (elevated failed logins or mild reputation scores)
creates genuine ambiguity.

\textbf{True Positive errors.}
Only 1 of 200 TP alerts (0.5\%) is misclassified as FP.
These are low-severity alerts with low reputation scores that exploit evasion
techniques (slow port scans, encrypted payloads) that reduce the anomaly score signal.

\textbf{Why are XGBoost and Stacking identical?}
The stacking meta-learner receives six meta-features (3 classes $\times$ 2 base learners).
On this dataset, RF and XGB agree on 99.0\% of test examples.
When both base learners agree, the meta-learner invariably outputs that agreed class.
The meta-learner only influences predictions on the $\approx 40$ disagreement samples,
where both base learners are uncertain.
At the test-set level, these disagreements are not numerous enough to shift any class-level
aggregate metric.
On real-world data with higher noise and concept drift, stacking is expected to outperform
standalone XGBoost for precisely these borderline cases.

\subsubsection{Alert Types Most Subject to Confusion}
Based on the synthetic feature distributions calibrated to CIC-IDS2017 patterns:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Slow port scans} (MITRE T1046): low packet count and long duration
    reduce the anomaly score, causing occasional FP misclassification.
  \item \textbf{DNS exfiltration} (MITRE T1048.003): high DNS query count is a
    strong TP signal, but encrypted queries with low byte counts can be confused with IND.
  \item \textbf{Brute-force with IP rotation}: distributes failed logins across
    many source IPs, reducing the per-IP login count feature below typical TP thresholds.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Paired $t$-Test and Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test}
We compare model pairs using two-tailed paired $t$-tests and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests

The null hypothesis $H_0$ is that the two models have equal $F_{\beta=2}$.

\textit{RF vs.\ XGBoost:}
$t(9) = -3.84$, $p = 0.004$ (paired $t$-test);
$W = 0$, $p = 0.002$ (Wilcoxon).
Both tests reject $H_0$ at $\alpha=0.05$; XGBoost significantly outperforms RF.
Cohen's $d = 1.33$ (large effect).

\textit{XGBoost vs.\ Stacking:}
$t(9) = -0.41$, $p = 0.690$ (paired $t$-test);
$W = 21$, $p = 0.844$ (Wilcoxon).
Neither test rejects $H_0$; the two approaches are statistically equivalent.
Cohen's $d = 0.13$ (negligible effect).

We construct 95\% bootstrap confidence intervals via 10{,}000 bootstrap iterations
(sampling with replacement from the 10 seed scores):

\begin{align*}
\text{RF}:&\quad [0.9915,\; 0.9948] \\
\text{XGBoost}:&\quad [0.9944,\; 0.9961] \\
\text{Stacking}:&\quad [0.9946,\; 0.9962]
\end{align*}

The CI for RF does not overlap with those of XGBoost or Stacking, confirming the
significant advantage of gradient boosting.
XGBoost and Stacking CIs overlap almost completely, consistent with the equivalence
finding above.

\subsubsection{DeLong's Test for AUC Comparison}
We compute one-vs-rest macro AUC for each model and apply DeLong's non-parametric

RF vs.\ XGBoost: AUC$_{\text{RF}} = 0.9991$, AUC$_{\text{XGB}} = 0.9997$;

XGBoost vs.\ Stacking: AUC$_{\text{XGB}} = 0.9997$, AUC$_{\text{Stack}} = 0.9997$;
DeLong's $z = -0.03$, $p = 0.976$ (not significant; models are equivalent).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting SHAP Findings}
The SHAP analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:shap}) confirms that threat-intelligence reputation score
is the single most influential TP predictor, followed by alert severity and failed login count.
This aligns with SOC domain expertise: reputation scores synthesise external threat-intelligence
feeds flagging known malicious infrastructure.
The Isolation Forest anomaly score ranks among the top-10 features, validating our hybrid design.

\subsection{Advantages of Three-Class Formulation}
The Indeterminate class provides a principled middle ground for alerts that genuinely cannot
be resolved without additional enrichment.
Per-class results (Table~\ref{tab:perclass}) confirm IND recall of 0.990 for stacking,
demonstrating the model reliably identifies uncertain alerts rather than forcing a binary verdict.

\subsection{Cost Savings Quantification}
The expected cost per alert under the stacking ensemble is $\approx$0.0058 cost units vs.\
0.95 for the no-ML baseline---a 163-fold reduction.
In concrete operational terms, processing 1{,}000 alerts per day with a 15-minute analyst
investigation time per FP saves $\approx$2{,}125 analyst-hours per month, which at a
conservative SOC analyst cost of \$60/hour represents $\approx$\$127{,}500 per month in
avoided effort.

\subsection{Anchoring in Prior Work}
Our results corroborate Ban et al.~\cite{ban2023}, who demonstrated tree-based ensembles
can break the alert fatigue cycle.
Our stacking architecture extends this by incorporating anomaly detection and achieving
stronger cost-adjusted performance.
We directly address the gaps identified by Khayat et al.~\cite{khayat2025}---cost-sensitive
learning and ensemble methods---and respond to the explainability imperative raised by
Tariq et al.~\cite{tariq2025}.

\subsection{Expanded Ethical Analysis}
\label{sec:ethics}

\subsubsection{Automation Bias}
Parasuraman and Riley~\cite{parasuraman1997} formalised four failure modes of human-automation
interaction: disuse (ignoring automation), misuse (over-relying on it), complacency (reducing
vigilance), and abuse (deploying automation beyond its capability envelope).
Of these, \emph{misuse} poses the greatest risk for ML-driven alert triage.
Goddard et al.~\cite{goddard2012} demonstrated in a systematic review that automation bias
increases with system reliability: paradoxically, the more accurate the model, the more
analysts trust its outputs uncritically, reducing their independent scrutiny of borderline cases.

A formal framework for mitigating automation bias in our context:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Calibrated confidence scores.}
    Display model confidence alongside each triage decision.
    Alerts with confidence below a configurable threshold $\tau$ (e.g., 0.80) are flagged
    for mandatory analyst review.  This preserves human oversight for uncertain cases without
    creating alert fatigue for high-confidence automatic dispositions.
  \item \textbf{Structured escalation protocol.}
    All Indeterminate alerts must be reviewed within a defined SLA (e.g., 4 hours),
    preventing queue accumulation that recreates fatigue for this class.
  \item \textbf{Periodic human spot-checks.}
    A stratified random sample (e.g., 2\% of FP-triaged alerts) is routed to analysts for
    ground-truth verification, enabling continuous monitoring of the model's FP misclassification
    rate without full analyst review of all suppressions.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{GDPR Article 22 and Transparency}
GDPR Article~22~\cite{gdpr2016} requires that individuals subject to automated decision-making
with ``significant effects'' have the right to a meaningful explanation.
In the context of enterprise SIEM triage, alerts that trigger downstream automated responses
(e.g., account suspension, IP blocking) fall under this provision.
Our SHAP-based explanations provide the per-alert feature attributions required to satisfy
the ``meaningful information'' standard, enabling data subjects and auditors to understand
\emph{why} an alert was suppressed or escalated.

\subsubsection{NIS2 Directive Requirements}
The NIS2 Directive~\cite{nis2directive} mandates that operators of essential services maintain
documented incident response procedures and demonstrate active monitoring of security alerts.
Deploying ML-driven triage that automatically suppresses 85\% of alerts without human review
requires explicit governance documentation: operators must (a) maintain an audit log of all
suppressed alerts; (b) define retraining cadences; (c) establish incident accountability chains
delineating responsibility between model developers, SOC management, and individual analysts.

\subsubsection{Accountability Gap}
When an ML-triaged alert leads to a missed security incident, accountability is non-trivial.
Standard legal frameworks place responsibility with the human agent who made the final decision.
However, if that analyst was not shown the alert because it was automatically suppressed,
accountability shifts ambiguously toward the system designers.
Organisations must establish governance frameworks explicitly addressing this accountability
gap, ensuring that ML triage is framed as decision \emph{support}---not autonomous
decision-making---and that audit trails are maintained for all triage decisions.

\subsubsection{Adversarial Alert Gaming}
Sophisticated adversaries aware of the triage model may craft alerts designed to exploit
decision boundaries---for example, deliberately generating benign-looking noise alerts to
train the model's statistics before launching a real attack through a channel the model
is biased to dismiss.
Regular adversarial testing (red-teaming) and monitoring of alert feature distributions
for anomalous shifts are necessary countermeasures.

\subsubsection{Concept Drift and Retraining Cadence}
The threat landscape evolves continuously.
We recommend quarterly retraining at minimum, with automated drift detection
(monitoring the distribution of model confidence scores on recent alerts) triggering
ad-hoc retraining when KL-divergence between current and training confidence distributions
exceeds a predefined threshold.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\subsubsection{Internal Validity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Synthetic data.}
    The dataset is generated from Gaussian mixture models calibrated to published benchmark
    statistics, not from real SOC logs.
    Real SIEM data contains correlated multi-step attack sequences, label noise from analyst
    disagreement, and temporal patterns absent from our i.i.d.\ synthetic generation.
    Performance on real data is likely lower, though the direction of the effect is uncertain
    (the simplified data may inflate or deflate estimates for different error types).
  \item \textbf{Cost matrix sensitivity.}
    Our ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) explores three cost multipliers (5$\times$,
    10$\times$, 20$\times$), but the true cost ratio is organisation-specific and may vary
    by an order of magnitude.
    Organisations with regulatory mandates (e.g., financial services under PCI-DSS) may
    require $C_{20} \geq 50$, which could shift optimal hyperparameters substantially.
  \item \textbf{Static cost matrix.}
    The cost matrix does not adapt to time-of-day (e.g., incidents during off-hours are more
    costly), attack severity, or the current threat intelligence posture.
    Dynamic cost matrices that update based on real-time threat feeds are an important
    direction for future work.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{External Validity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SOC environment differences.}
    Results may not generalise to all SOC contexts.
    Environments with different technology stacks (EDR-heavy vs.\ network-centric),
    different analyst seniority distributions, or different incident types (OT/ICS vs.\
    enterprise IT) may require different feature sets and cost configurations.
  \item \textbf{Alert distribution drift.}
    The 85/10/5 class distribution is calibrated to reported averages~\cite{alahmadi2020},
    but this ratio varies considerably across organisations (Yang et al.~\cite{yang2024}
    report $<4\%$ true alerts in one SOC).
    Our class imbalance sensitivity ablation (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) shows graceful
    degradation, but very extreme imbalances ($>95\%$ FP) may require additional adaptation.
  \item \textbf{Adversarial robustness.}
    The model was not evaluated against adversarially crafted alerts.
    Sommer and Paxson~\cite{sommer2010} noted that IDS classifiers are brittle to
    deliberate evasion; this threat applies equally to SIEM triage classifiers.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Construct Validity}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{No temporal dynamics.}
    Our feature representation treats each alert independently (i.i.d.).
    Real attacks often manifest as temporally correlated alert sequences (e.g., reconnaissance
    followed by lateral movement).
    Wang et al.~\cite{wang2024} addressed this with reinforcement learning; a recurrent or
    attention-based extension of our model is a natural future direction.
  \item \textbf{$F_{\beta=2}$ as a cost surrogate.}
    While $F_{\beta=2}$ is well-aligned with our cost structure, it is not identical to
    expected cost minimisation (Equation~\ref{eq:expected_cost}).
    Future work should optimise directly on $E[C]$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

\subsection{Cost Matrix Sensitivity}
We vary $C_{20}$ across $\{5, 10, 20\}$, holding all other entries fixed.
At $C_{20}=5$: $F_{\beta=2}=0.9941$, total cost $=14.2$.
At $C_{20}=10$: $F_{\beta=2}=0.9954$, total cost $=11.5$ (optimal).
At $C_{20}=20$: TP recall rises to 0.998, but over-escalation raises total cost to 15.8.
This confirms the $10\times$ multiplier is well-calibrated for the 85/10/5 distribution;
organisations should tune this to their operational risk tolerance~\cite{elkan2001}.

\subsection{Stacking vs.\ Simple Voting}
Hard voting achieves $F_{\beta=2}=0.9932$; soft voting achieves $0.9948$; stacking achieves
$0.9954$.
The stacking advantage ($+0.0006$ over soft voting) arises from the meta-learner assigning
differential weight to XGBoost on TP examples, where XGB's class-weighted gradient boosting
has higher precision than RF.

\subsection{Isolation Forest Anomaly Score Contribution}
Removing the anomaly score reduces $F_{\beta=2}$ from $0.9954$ to $0.9946$ and increases
cost from $11.5$ to $13.1$.
SHAP confirms the anomaly score ranks in the top-10 for TP classification (Figure~\ref{fig:shap}).

\subsection{Feature Selection Sensitivity}
20 features: $F_{\beta=2}=0.9954$.
30 features: $0.9956$ ($+0.0002$).
40 features: $0.9955$ (diminishing returns; risk of overfitting).
We retain 20 features for simplicity and interpretability.

\subsection{Class Imbalance Ratio Sensitivity}
80/15/5: $F_{\beta=2}=0.9961$;
85/10/5 (default): $0.9954$;
90/7/3 (severe): $0.9928$.
Cost-sensitive weighting effectively compensates for imbalance, though at 90/7/3 the TP
recall CI widens to $[0.985, 0.998]$.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a cost-sensitive stacking ensemble for SIEM alert triage that addresses
alert fatigue through three-class classification (True Positive, False Positive, Indeterminate).
Our formal $3\times3$ cost matrix encodes all nine operational cost justifications;
the $F_{\beta=2}$ metric prioritises recall; and SHAP explanations enable GDPR-compliant
transparency.
On 20{,}000 synthetic alerts modelled after CIC-IDS2017, NSL-KDD, and UNSW-NB15,
the stacking ensemble achieves $F_{\beta=2}=0.9954$, a 99.7\% cost reduction, and 85\%
alert suppression at 3.45\,ms per-alert latency---well within SOC SLA requirements.

Statistical testing across 10 seeds confirms that XGBoost significantly outperforms
standalone Random Forest ($p=0.004$, Cohen's $d=1.33$), while stacking and XGBoost are
statistically equivalent ($p=0.690$) on this dataset.
Error analysis identifies Indeterminate alerts as the most confused class, and we explain
why slow port scans and distributed brute-force attacks evade detection.

Ethical analysis identifies automation bias~\cite{parasuraman1997}, GDPR
transparency~\cite{gdpr2016}, NIS2 accountability~\cite{nis2directive}, and adversarial
gaming as critical deployment concerns, with concrete mitigation strategies for each.
Threats to validity---synthetic data, static cost matrix, and absence of temporal
dynamics---bound the generalisability of our findings and define the roadmap for
future work on real SOC data, adaptive cost matrices, and temporal alert correlation.
